**The Shower Strategy**  
_Theme: Blue Ocean Strategy (5AM showers)_  
[Chapter Four: None of it Works Unless You Do]  
I had one goal as a young boy. I grew up in a very religious home. Young men in my faith are expected to serve as missionaries for two years, if they are at all able, at age 19. (Now they can serve at 18. Young women may also serve.) That is what I wanted to do. At eight years old my parents opened a savings account for me and one for my older sister. I saved every bit of the money I earned for that far off mission I would someday serve.  
Receiving one’s mission call is an exciting time. Once an application is submitted candidates wait for their call that could assign them almost anywhere in the world. I was more than excited to learn that I would be at the Missionary Training Center (MTC) for ten weeks to learn Spanish and then off to serve in the Costa Rica, San Jose Mission for two years. As a missionary, I quickly became known as one who followed the rules, even in the MTC. (Missionaries are assigned partners called companions. Male missionaries use the title "Elder" and female missionaries are called "Sister".) It helped immensely to have a companion at the MTC as dedicated to the mission as I was. The first day at the MTC our instructor asked which of us had taken any Spanish classes. The 11 other missionaries in my district raised their hands. I was the only one with no Spanish schooling. This frightened and motivated me. My companion and I would get up at 5:00 am, one hour before the required 6:00 am wake up time. We did this for a couple reasons. We lived on third floor of a building where all missionaries got up at the same time every day. Which meant that, hundreds of people were trying to use the showers at the same time. Besides being crowded, the hot water would quickly run out, especially on the top floor. You could actually hear when the water went cold by floor as the yips moved down the building of those caught with their pants down. Once all these young men showered and dressed, they all went to breakfast… at the same time. The line rapped the building hall, outside, and down the sidewalk. Elder Welling and I discovered that by waking up early, we could enjoy a hot shower in an un-crowded bathroom, get dressed, and be down to breakfast as the doors opened, sparring us the wait in line. This opened the floodgates to not just the hour of extra time for getting up early but almost an entire additional hour to study. And what we felt we needed to study most was Spanish.  
*Time is a limited resource. If you are vigilant you can find better ways to maximize this critical commodity. The program for learning a foreign language at the MTC includes the critical element SYL, Speak Your Language, or HSI, Habla Su Idioma, in Spanish. Elder Welling and I determined to do just that. It was hard, and caused some contention when others were not as strict as us with the program, but ultimately our hard work paid off. Those who expressed that they would learn the language when they got to country were disappointed that it didn’t get any easier. Many of them, even after two years, did not have a command of the language. The best advice I received before entering the MTC was from my good buddy Erik, the guy who talked me into joining the Army. He was older than me by a few months and passed on a few simple, but powerful, words of wisdom in a letter from where he served in the Philippines. "When you leave for the MTC you are not leaving for Costa Rica" he wrote. That seemed obvious enough. He continued "I left so excited to get to the Philippines that I missed some great opportunities to learn." I was fortunate to learn from someone else and I did not share the same regret.  
This is a hard lesson to learn for a motivated leader. Patience is part of it, but it is not simply the ability to wait it out. Leaders must take advantage of situations to the fullest. Corporate training, for example, can appear to be a waste of time when you have "real work" to do. Avoid the urge and tendency to blow it off. Take advantage, it is worth it. The other lesson is that, if you have feedback don’t keep it to yourself. Share it with those to your left and right. If you get feedback, pay attention. Making mistakes is costly. Making mistakes after you are given the answers, is costly and dumb.  

**Week 13 - Apr 15: Misionero Excelente**  
_Theme: High Expectations & The Pygmalion Effect_  
[Chapter Four: Part 2]  
For my first assignment in Costa Rica, I found myself paired with a missionary from Honduras. It is typical for missionaries to have a companion missionary to work alongside. My companion would teach me everything from where to get breakfast and catch the bus, to how to teach and serve with love, but he did not speak English and, as much as I had studied, I truly did not speak Spanish. It would be a challenging experience. In addition to miles of walking and talking to hundreds of people, Elder Ventura drilled me constantly on my gringo accent. “Roca, piedra, turunca,” he repeated. I attempted again and again. Before long, locals began to ask where I was from. I didn’t always understand their questions nor was my vocabulary truly up to the point that I could explain it when I did understand, but my accent had improved to the point that many people were convinced that I was a native Spanish speaker. That is until they heard me speak more and my limited vocabulary and faulty grammar gave me away.  
The President the mission interviewed all of the missionaries one on one every couple of months to gage their wellbeing and provide individual counsel. In my first such interview about a month after I arrived the President started something like this “Elder Sparrow," he said looking me in the eyes, "eres un buen misionaro” (Elder being my missionary title.) “Elder Sparrow, you are a good missionary” he complemented me in Spanish. “Gracias presidente” I said back with my fresh gringo accent, feeling pretty good about myself. He looked at me, squinted his eyes slightly, and brought his eyebrows closer together as he shook his head from side to side “pero no eres un misionaro excelente. Nesesito que seas un misionero excelente. Entiendes?” (”But you are not an excellent missionary. I need you to be an excellent missionary. Do you understand?”) I shook my head up and down. I did understand, but I was devastated. He proceeded to provide me with some specific areas to improve. I understood basic principles but lacked a deeper knowledge of scriptural text and doctrine. I obeyed the mission rules but fell short of keeping my thoughts and actions consistent with a representative of a supreme power. President Gonzalez gave me some specific areas to study and actions to demonstrate maturity. That verbal gut punch motivated me to work harder and be better. My dad had told me in the first letter he wrote to me at the MTC “There are many motivational words of encouragement but none of them work unless you do”. I determined to work hard and become a misionaro excelente.  
Don’t be afraid to let someone know when they need to step up their game. Slackers and high performers alike respond to sincere, directed, constructive feedback. Those who do not respond to constructive feedback fall into the category discussed previously that should be removed from the organization as to not drag down the team. This is of course a balance. I am not at all suggesting the constant pointing out of faults and specifically discourage belittling, yelling, or attempting to motivate through passive aggression. Complements and encouragement are better motivators but we often think that because we see the specific deficiencies that they must be obvious to the one committing them. Shown the path to success most of us will be happy to get on that road.  

**Week 14 - Apr 22: Leading from the Right**  
_Theme: Humility & Leading while following (Elder Searls)_  
[Chapter Four: Part 3]  
I moved to my second area and with five months in the mission field I became the senior companion. My junior companion, Elder Searls, had been there but a month and had disliked much of it. He spoke with a bit of a drawl that came through his budding Spanish. People didn't always understand his words but they could feel the sincerity from his heart the size of Texas, where he was from. On my first day with him I had him show me everyone and everything he knew. At the end of a long day we walked back to the concrete box we called home. As we passed a particular house he said, "I think we should talk to the people at that house". “OK” I said and I walked toward it. “Right now?” he asked astonished. “Did you have another time in mind?” I asked rhetorically. We stood to the gate looking at each other for a bit. I finally said, as if he should have already known, “Well, it’s your house.” He tapped on the gate with a large coin and hollered “Upe”; as is costmary in Costa Rica. A woman came to the door and introduced herself as Joana. Together we set an appointment when we could return to share a message with her. Elder Searls floated home that evening. When his feet did touch the ground, they danced every step. That day I had allowed him to be the teacher, to show me. I hadn’t done it because I read it in a book, which now you have, so you have no excuse not to. I had done it because I was able to admit that, just because I was in charge didn’t mean that I knew everything, which was easy because I knew hardly anything. Not knowing is not weak for a leader. Not learning is weak. And not using the knowledge of those you lead is silly. You will be a better leader when you listen to, and learn from, the wealth of knowledge held by those you lead.  
A month later Elder Searls left the area and Elder Stoker, strait from Washington, arrived. His thick beard that darkened even a freshly shaved face didn't hide how young he was. He wanted desperately to be a good missionary but he was inexperienced, nervous, and very homesick. I had not realized how much basic training had prepared me not to worry too much about being homesick. I never worried much about moving to a new place. I didn't know how to relate. So I did what I knew how to do: work hard, and follow the rules. Elder Stoker and I worked hard. We literally ran at times in slacks and ties to get where we needed to be. That didn’t leave much time for doing much else, including being homesick. I learned that I work harder and I strive to be better when someone is watching and learning from me. This may not be the case for everyone but when I am running alone I am much more prone to slow my pace or shorten the distance without someone there to hold me accountable. I am especially more likely to step up my game if a young person is running with me that I feel is watching me as an example. What I didn’t appreciate at that time was the reality of depression and anxiety. While I am happy it worked out, and I am a big proponent that hard work can solve a lot of problems, it is important to recognize in your subordinates, peers, and even superiors mental states that may require professional assistance.  
Missionaries typically have a handful of companions over the course of their mission. I had 16. I generally get along with others and figured I could be companions with anyone. But in my first zone conference there was a missionary in attendance who seemed hyper, spastic, and borderline unprofessional. I thought to myself, “I hope I’m never companions with that guy.” Well, sure enough, fate, irony, and a God with a sense of humor, or a need to teach a young punk a lesson, could not pass up a chance to make that happen. Elder Carrias was from Guatemala. He was an odd duck, but he had a big heart and truly cared about people. We eventually learned to work very well together and I learned to appreciate his enthusiasm.  
Not everyone thinks, looks, or acts the way you do. That does not make them a poor candidate for your team. Diversity and an open mind can lead to unique solutions. Shared vision and effort on all parts go a long way toward understanding and ultimately success.  
In the mean time, I wrote home to my father for advice and guidance. He served as a missionary in England 20 years earlier and I hoped for some words of inspiration and encouragement. What I got was a letter that made me so angry that I did not write to him directly for months. He told me that he was concerned that I was not receiving enough trials to shape me into the man he knew I could be. He confessed that he had been praying that I would have hardships and trials that would challenge me. “Well thanks a lot Dad”, I thought. It would be many months before I could forgive him for wishing ill upon me, as I saw it. What I realize now, is that the difficult things we pass through in life make us stronger… and they make for good stories. Much like lifting weights builds muscles, challenges builds character and resilience.  

**Week 15 - Apr 29: The Adult in the Room**  
_Theme: Emotional Maturity (Elder Bell)_  
[Chapter Four: Part 4]  
After serving for nine months, my mission president called me to be a district leader, responsible for several missionary companionships. Two months later he called me to be a zone leader; this came with responsibility over several districts. Mission presidents typically have one or two missionary assistants who over-see all the zone leaders in the entire mission.1 While not applied for, or generally sought after positions, it is an honor and privilege to be an assistant to the president (or AP). I served as zone leader for 3 months when Elder Hicks, the former assistant to the president, came to be my companion for the last few months of his mission. I never mentioned it to Elder Hicks, but I felt like the president sent him to groom me to be the next AP. I felt like I was fast tracking to the top. A few months later I had the humbling experience of being called to be a district leader again. This brought with it the most challenging times in my mission. I was paired, on two occasions, with missionaries who were not as motivated about the work as I was. It can be difficult to be away from home. It can be difficult to talk to people. It can be difficult to do the same work and get much of the same rejection every day for two years. But that rejection and work and longing can be softened and made bearable by having someone with whom to share the burden. Loneliness can be a great challenge even when you are surrounded by others; even when you are in charge. Loneliness is real.  
Lesson re-learned, eventually: sometimes moving to positions "in charge" of more people is not the best way to use your skills. I had the opportunity to train more new missionaries, which was, by far, the most rewarding times for me. As mentioned before, I worked harder knowing others were looking to me as the example. Again, promotions do not always equal being the boss of more people. Connected is the fact that reward does not always equal dollars. While money is an important motivator. Most of us are motivated by doing what we are educated, trained, good at, and hired to do.  
Elder Joshua Bell was athletic, tall, handsome, charismatic, likable, and many other things I only dreamt of being. He had turned down an opportunity to play professional baseball to be a missionary and as his first companion and trainer I felt responsible to make his sacrifice worthwhile, but he required little coaching. He was naturally outgoing, a skilled teacher, and even his Spanish was progressing impressively. We worked hard and had a lot of fun. Late one evening at the end of a particularly unsuccessful day of canceled appointments and rejection, we knocked on the door of a gentleman whose curt attitude didn't help the mood. Elder Bell returned the sentiment, being a little short with him. I followed suit. The door closed and I stated the obvious, "Well, that didn't go well." Elder Bell looked at me with disappointment in his eyes, "You didn't do anything to make it better" he retorted. I got a little defensive. "That was your door" I shot back. "Yes, But you are the trainer" He chastised me. Saying that I should have made an effort to change the tone or at least leave the man at the door with a good impression and a pleasant experience rather than leaving on a sour note. It was a long quiet walk home from the far edge of our area as I tasted humble pie for the whole 45 minutes. He was right and I made sure he knew, that I knew it, when we arrived at our doorstep.  
You are the leader. You don't have to be a "party pooper" or a "fun hater" but you do have to be the adult in the room. This has been a particularly difficult lesson for me at times when I desired to join the pity party and act like a martyr. While I may desire to pout like a two year old or blame everyone but myself like a teenager, as a competent capable adult, I have to remind myself that, it may take work but I, especially as a leader, possess the power to make situations better.  
With the end of my two year mission visibly approaching on the calendar I reflected on the attitude and effort, in their final days, of the missionaries who I had seen depart ahead of me. I determined that on my last day I would say “goodbye, I’m going back home.” and I wanted them to be shocked and unsuspecting because I had never mentioned how close I was to going home. This is a tiny note that will likely be insignificant to most readers, but for me, this is a major part of who I aspire to be. I want to be known as someone who runs through the finish line, fights to the end, doesn’t quit, is not a giver-upper.
