## Scott Core

### The Early Days

My earliest memories are of school. One of the first is preschool, running around in circles and singing:

> "See me run,  
> running around in circles,  
> running and running,  
> running around the room."

Songs are powerful memory anchors. Vivid images, smells, and emotions bind themselves to memory in ways that make them easy to recall forever. I remember a kid named Cody eating my cousin Andy's glue, and the memory comes with all of that attached.

I learned a lot from Mrs. Kathy at Kerr Preschool. I worked hard, but I did not know I was working hard. I simply enjoyed it.

**Lesson learned:** work hard and enjoy it.

That theme comes up again and again in my life. I have never been the smartest, strongest, most attractive, or most social person in the office, but being someone others can count on because they know you will contribute meaningful effort makes you valuable.

### First Grade: Kindness Matters

I do not remember much about first grade except that my teacher, Mrs. Terman, quickly became known as Mrs. "Termite." Hilarious if you are a six-year-old boy. Less hilarious if you are the one being called names.

"Scotty Watty needs to go Potty" sounds ridiculous now, but it devastated my six-year-old self.

**Lesson learned:** be kind.

No one likes to be called names or belittled. It seems simple, but many leaders still miss it. Bullying, hazing, sexist comments, racial slurs, and ethnic innuendos are not funny. Do not do it. Do not laugh at it. Do not tolerate it.

### Third Grade: Do Not Waste Time

In third grade I discovered something about myself that I did not fully understand for years: I do not like busy work.

I realized that sometimes the work was not there to teach. It was there to keep us busy and quiet. No one likes having their time wasted, not even an eight-year-old.

**Lesson learned:** do not waste people's time.

People will do extraordinary things if they believe their effort matters. They resent being managed as if their time is disposable.

### Keyboarding, Why, and Mastery

In seventh grade I took keyboarding and discovered copy and paste in WordPerfect. I finished my assignments in a third of the time and spent the rest of class playing _Scorched Earth_ from a floppy disk.

What I did not master was typing.

By the time I understood the purpose of all that repetition, it was too late. Even now, pecking at keys while I write, I wish I had understood the intent behind the task earlier. I could have met the real objective instead of just gaming the assignment.

### The Farm Years and the Spoonful of Sugar

I worked hard on the farm. Before I was even ten, I rode my bike a mile and a half to the barn to milk cows. We made games out of the ride. We named our bikes. Mine was called "Predator," mostly because that was written on the handlebars.

I would call the race like a sports announcer:

> "Karen on Rainbow-Rider is lagging behind, but here she comes with her second wind.  
> She's passing Schwinn.  
> She's coming around Husky.  
> It's neck and neck with Predator.  
> Photo finish. Rainbow-Rider wins by a tire width."

Games, songs, stories, and water fights made the work bearable.

Yes, it is cheesy. Yes, it is corny. But as Mary Poppins sang, a spoonful of sugar helps the medicine go down.

### The Pillow Incident

Around age ten, our family stayed in a cabin for Thanksgiving. Stormy weather trapped eight rowdy kids inside. No toys. No video games. A pillow war was inevitable.

One throw sent a pillow into the overhead light fixture. Smoke began to billow. Panic followed. Adults scrambled for ladders and long objects.

In the chaos, I calmly walked to the light switch and turned it off.

Silence. Smoke cleared. Then laughter.

**Lesson learned:** sometimes the solution is simple, and sometimes it comes from the person no one expected.

Listen to everyone, and do not be afraid to offer the simple solution yourself.

### Chapter Close

One of the biggest lessons that stayed with me from all of this is that if I shift my focus from myself to the person in front of me, life almost always goes better.

Once I am trying to understand them, I almost always care about them more. Once it is clear to them that I care, something changes. They lean in.

Being kind, working hard and enjoying it, not wasting people's time, and listening are not just nice ideas. They are critical to life, work, and the internal world we carry around with us.

### Working Sidebar: Effort + Joy

Steven Kotler, in _The Rise of Superman_, describes flow as living in that narrow channel between boredom and anxiety, and he shares a wonderfully specific rule of thumb: flow tends to show up when the challenge is about 4% beyond your current skill level. Stretch without panic.

Daniel Pink's _Drive_ explains why that feels so good: when people have autonomy, mastery, and purpose, motivation stops feeling like force and starts feeling like momentum.

Preschool is secretly perfect for this. The stakes are low, the curiosity is high, and everything is just a little bit new. You are working hard without feeling like you are working.

### Working Sidebar: Psychological Safety and Purpose

Google's Project Aristotle found that the strongest predictor of team performance was not intelligence or experience. It was psychological safety: an environment where people feel safe to speak without fear of humiliation.

That is why first-grade ridicule matters beyond childhood memory. Kindness is not sentimental. It is structural.

Self-Determination Theory makes the same point from another angle. Humans are motivated by autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Busy work violates autonomy. It quietly tells people their time is not valuable. People will do extraordinary things when the work matters and resent ordinary things when it does not.

### Working Sidebar: Meaning, Play, and Endurance

In _Man's Search for Meaning_, Viktor Frankl observed that those who could attach meaning to suffering endured it better. I would not compare childhood farm work to Frankl's experience, but extremity often strips away the lesson that matters: story transforms labor into purpose, and purpose can sometimes even bring joy to difficulty.

Naming the bike. Calling the race. Creating drama. That was not just distraction. It was resilience.

### Closing Reflection

I was a pretty awkward kid. I know, I know, I am a pretty awkward adult too. But in spite of the struggles and anxiety that has caused at times, I am grateful for the lessons I learned from being awkward and self-conscious.

The biggest one is this: no matter how weird I feel in a situation, if I shift the focus from myself to others, it almost always goes better. Once I am focused on the person in front of me and trying to understand them, I almost always like them. Once it is clear to them that I genuinely care about them and what they are saying, something magic happens. They lean in.

Like all of the lessons in this chapter, this one is embarrassingly simple. If you pay attention to the person in front of you instead of yourself, you begin to understand them. If you understand them, you will probably care about them. Then you are much more likely to treat them the way they want to be treated.

Olivia Fox Cabane, in _The Charisma Myth_, breaks charisma down into power, presence, and warmth. If you are with someone, pay attention to them, be present with them, and try to understand them. As you understand them, you care more. That creates warmth.

Being kind is not just a nice thing to do. It is critical to life, work, and even to your internal world.
