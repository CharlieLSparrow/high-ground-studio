### Character: Tip and the Winnemucca Promise

As my siblings and I played in the barnyard on a summer day, the dry summer air seemed to blow in an unknown friendly dog from out of nowhere. She was a hyper pup with long hair, a long nose, and a perpetually happy face. She was no Sheppard, Collie, or Sheepdog of any kind. Clearly not a working dog at all, definitely nothing like the red healer, Bandit, we had that could bring the cows in from the corral for milking with one “get ‘em”. She was clearly a stray, and clearly a mutt.

But when my little brother played the puppy dog eyes on my dad, Dad agreed that we could run an ad in the paper and if no one claimed the thing, Charlie could keep it. When after weeks of asking around and running ads proved fruitless, the promise to keep the pup was honored and Dad gave in. A black end to her all white tail gave her the name Tip, or Tippie, as Aunt Dena Rae called her, and she became part of the family.

Then, one day, Tip was nowhere to be found. She was not at the barn, not at Grandma's house, not in the fields or the pasture, or at the neighbor down the dusty road. She had disappeared as suddenly as she had appeared. Kids cried and prayed.

One night about a week after Tip was lost my little brother made an odd request. “Dad,” he pleaded with the same puppy eyes he used to keep the dog in the first place, “will you go get Tippie no matter how far away she is.” “Of course,” the answer came back quickly.

The very next day the phone rang. A woman from the pound in Winnemucca, Nevada had found a dog with a tag indicating it belonged to Charlie Sparrow. Winnemucca is a seven hour drive from Weston, Idaho, one way.

What was a guy to do? He could have ignored the call. He could have told the nice lady on the phone from Winnemucca, “Ma’am, you just go ahead and take off those tags that say he belongs to Charlie Sparrow and give that hyper, long haired, mutt to some other nice kid”. He could have found a much better dog as a replacement. He could have, but instead, he didn't hesitate. He got in his truck and drove for 14 hours to get it.

That dog was queen of the house after that. But the real hero was Dad. My father was a man who kept promises. I never liked that dog. My dad certainly didn’t drive to Nevada for me, and I never asked him to. But that day I knew he would. I knew he could be trusted “no matter how far” he had to go.

**Leaders who are trusted are leaders who are followed.**

### Standards + Forgiveness

We had a small plot of 20 acres behind the house and my grandpa had 40 acres behind his place. With the 30 acres we leased from the neighbors, it kept a boy with some work to do in the summer. Dad used to say things like, “cow’s don’t care if you’re busy or tired.” “They don’t care if it’s Saturday or a holiday or your birthday.” “They have to be milked, twice a day, every day.” “There are no days off”.

My dad trusted my sister and me with incredible responsibility for pre-teen adolescence. For the most part we made him proud, but we did have our moments. I remember one evening when the milking was entirely completed, I went into the wash room to remove the line from the tank. The line was already removed and I knew immediately by the milk splashed around the drain that it had not been recently removed by my sister. We had literally dumped the entire milking down the drain.

My dad was rightfully upset when we called him over the CB radio to let him know what had happened. But I was surprised at how quickly we were forgiven and confidence was restored. It was clearly accidental. He truly trusted us and he needed us.

Leaders have a responsibility to remove from their organization individuals who have a habit of doing things that are not beneficial and/or detrimental to the organization. But individuals who make mistakes and work hard to correct and reduce mistakes should be forgiven. This creates an atmosphere of honesty. When mistakes are made, which will happen, it is important to have a climate where it can be confessed wholly and honestly.

**Forgive and Fire, both are critical.**

### Habits, Grudges, and Communication

Work on the farm was an everyday affair. Cows had to get milked every day, twice a day, no exceptions. It would be many years later that I would understand that greatness is typically forged, not in one great act, but from meaningful, often monotonous, things done every day. The key is to **build habits**, to be consistent, but know when to deviate.

I harbored ill feeling towards my father for a long time for all the things I thought he should have provided me, and for all the wisdom I thought he should have imparted to me. I understand now that he did his best and I love and appreciate him for that.

**Holding grudges and blaming others for doing something, or not doing something, is not helpful. Grudges lead only to mistrust and contention.**

Unfortunately, **communication** is not some silver bullet or magic solution to all problems, nor the answer to all leadership challenges, but there is a reason it is included in nearly all leadership courses. It is an important and powerful tool. If you haven’t noticed by now, there are people on this planet, and to be a leader, other people will be involved.

### Attention: Hold the Light

I remember one cold winter night looking down into a dark hole, which I believe was the water box at the spring that supplied water to our house. The water running right out of the frozen ground was only not frozen itself because it was in motion. The pump had stopped, probably because of the cold.

I stood over the hole looking down at my dad who hung onto a cold aluminum ladder with the inside of his elbow in order to free his hands to work. My job should have been simple, hold the flashlight. But despite the simplicity of the task I was not doing a great job. I was having trouble because I was trying to tell some tall tale that required demonstrating what was happening in the story as much as telling it. My flailing arms were not holding consistent light on the frozen pump.

My dad eventually took the light from me to hold under his chin while he continued to work with both hands. I was grateful because my hand was getting cold from holding the light and I was having a hard time making the exaggerated hand motions necessary for the dramatic nature of my story with the flashlight in my hand.

At some point -20* F temperature was too much to even stand and flail my arms. I climbed in the truck and cranked up the heat, leaving him alone in the dark frozen hole struggling to get water to his family. Sorry dad. Thanks for listening.

Sometimes the best investment you can make is to **listen**.

### Invest in Them Your Time

Working summer months for the West Cache Canal Company required long hours of driving down bumpy dirt roads along the canal banks regulating the water flow and insuring everyone took their allotted turn. My dad often invited me to join him; which I was glad to do.

On such occasions, it was my job to periodically jump out of the truck to open the gates of the farmers along the route. Then after my dad drove the truck through, I would quickly close the gate, jump back in the truck, and we would again be on our way. The time was passed listening to the radio and drinking lemon-aid. Most of the time passed without conversation and most of the conversations we did have were not thought provoking or earth shattering but it left the window open to have important conversations.

Your time is valuable but if you don’t give any of that time to work alongside those you lead you will miss out on deeply understanding your subordinates and gaining their trust.

**Invest in them your time.**

### Warmth + Correction

My parents gave me great autonomy and freedom to come and go, as long as they knew where I was. I was a good kid, very naïve, but a good kid. And I didn't get into trouble. I did push my autonomy too far.

One night, after a show, Cindy and I hung out behind the theater in the parking lot long after the curtain closed. Before the days of cell phones when a quick “where are you” message could relieve worry, I was oblivious to the concern building in my house because I had not returned home.

Suddenly a car pulled into the dark, quiet parking lot. Two headlights shined on us like two thieves caught in the act. My dad pulled up next to us, stepped out, quickly looked at me with tired upset eyes and said, “Your mom is worried about you.” He looked at Cindy and softened his tone a little, but still quite sternly stated “your parents are probably worried about you too.”

That’s all he said. That’s all he had to say. It was after 2:00 am. Message received, we quickly said goodbye and went home.

**Leaders, give subordinates the freedom to succeed but you still have to care and make corrections when necessary. Subordinates, give your leader reason to trust you.**

### Character

The most powerful lesson Dad ever taught me is this:

> “It’s just what we do.”

It doesn’t sound like much at first. There’s no speech attached to it, no big defining moment. It just sort of… sits there.

And then, over time, it starts explaining things.

I’ve thought about that drive to Winnemucca more than I expected. Not in big reflective moments, but in the middle of normal life. Driving somewhere. Doing dishes. Something small happens, and the thought just shows up:

> Oh right… he actually did that.

Fourteen hours. For a dog who, in strictly farm terms, was more of a lifestyle decision than a contributor.

But that’s the part that makes the story stick. Not the effort. The ease of it. For him, it wasn’t a story. It was just what you do when you’ve said you’ll do something.

Character isn’t built in the moments we’d like it to be. It’s built in the small ones… the ones that don’t feel like anything at the time. Until they do.

### Standards + Forgiveness

There is a very specific kind of silence that follows a mistake you cannot undo.

You look at what just happened, and there isn’t a version of the story where you come out looking especially capable.

This was that.

A whole tank of milk is gone. Not misplaced. Not reduced. Gone in a way that suggests you may need to reconsider your relationship with responsibility.

That’s why I’ve always liked the line from *Moneyball*:

> “It’s a process. It’s a process. It’s a process.”

One moment is noise. A pattern is signal.

Scott’s story draws that line cleanly.

- A mistake is an event.
- A pattern is a trajectory.

They made a mistake. They were not the mistake.

And that distinction is what made it possible to tell the truth in the first place.

### Attention + Time

I like the flashlight story because it’s funny… and a little uncomfortable.

Scott is up top, telling a story. Dad is down in a frozen hole, trying to fix something important. And the flashlight is… doing its best.

Which feels about right.

Not because we’ve all stood over a well in Idaho winter, but because we’ve all been in that posture. Close to the work. Caring about the work. Just slightly misaligned with what’s actually needed.

We’re near it.  
We’re engaged.  
We’re just not quite helping.

The job was simple:

> Hold the light.

Steady. Where it’s needed.

And that turns out to be a bigger idea than it sounds.

### Warmth + Correction

At some point, it occurred to me that I wasn’t just enjoying Sherlock.

I was enjoying Watson.

The dependable one. The one who stays.

What people need is someone who shows up. Who listens. Who stays steady when things get a little sideways.

That’s why the parking lot moment works.

Dad doesn’t give a speech. He doesn’t escalate. He simply says, “Your mom is worried about you.”

Not loud… precise.

It lands, not because the sentence is extraordinary, but because everything behind it is: the relationship, the trust, the consistency.
