This book is not the first nor last book on leadership. It’s probably not the best book on leadership.  I wrote it for myself. I wrote it to document my life. It is my story. The book began more like a journal. I suppose I started writing a memoir before I knew what a memoir was, and long before they were popular. It turned into a book on leadership when I realized that much of life is about learning, and much of what I have learned in my life is how to teach and how to lead.  At times it will read more like a life-sketch. Others times it may feel more similar to a self-help book about leadership. At times things may seem out of order or out of context, but life is that way. Some lessons are learned quickly, like, don’t eat glue - learned in Chapter One. Other lessons take a lifetime and are not realized until the pieces of life are put together and the picture in the puzzle becomes clear.  Most lessons repeat themselves; sometimes as reminders and sometimes we finally understand for the first time. As you will read, many of the things I’ve learned, were learned “the hard way”, from making mistakes, learning from those mistakes, and bouncing back. I write honestly about my failings and hope that you can learn, and not make the same mistakes. You will make mistakes, and I will continue to error and fall short, but learning from your history and the history of others, striving for something better, and leaving a legacy, are the rewards of this life. I encourage you to write your own story, even if only for yourself. Write it down. You may discover that you’ve learned more along life’s road than you realized. And, someone else may benefit, if only to look and say, “Well, at least I don’t have his challenges” or “at least I didn’t do that”. I make no apologies for the writing style, only explanations. Besides, it’s my story and I’ll tell it however I want.

If you will not be forgotten after you are dead and rotten either write things worth the reading or do things worth the writing of –Benjamin Franklin
</ManuscriptBlock>

On Sundays at my house, growing up in Southeast Idaho, my mom, a small, thin woman with light skin but dark eyes and short dark hair, would get out of bed at 5:00 a.m. (sleeping in, compared to the rest of the week for her). At that hour she prepared roast and potatoes for Sunday dinner - not a big surprise when you have dairy cows and potato fields, but a luxury we were not allowed to take for granted at any rate. After three hours of church and before the cows had to be milked again, I would sit around the dark wood table on big wooden chairs with my two sisters, my brother, and mom. My dad at the head of the table would ask each of us, in turn, what we learned at church that day. It was at those times that he often attempted to impart words of fatherly wisdom to his progeny.

One day as we sat around that table in the house on bend in the road, with the rust colored linoleum beneath our feet, he told of a small swallow he saw earlier that week ferociously defending her nest from a red-tailed hawk. I stared out the big dining room window across the hay field with hay laying in neat rows. I lost track of the story as I watched birds of all sizes discovering rodents, bugs, and other feasts newly exposed by the freshly cut crop. His story complete, he paused. The quiet pulled me from my daydream. I had missed the point of the tale. I suppose the moral of that particular story was less important than what he shared next. He looked passed me sitting at the foot of the table across from him, passed the golden Formica countertops behind me, passed the classic gold refrigerator of that late 70s home, through the door, through carport, and out into the pastures beyond. Then he encouraged us to look for life lessons in everyday things. It seemed pretty simple for such dramatic effect. Intrigued, and always looking to please/impress my dad, I watched for months but saw nothing. I tried to come up with things randomly. This banana peel is like when you… get… old… or… brown spots… that… or… even throw it away. I got nothing. I figured I just didn’t see things the way my dad did. Discouraged, I almost gave up entirely. Then one night I went to see a symphony and concert at the Assembly Hall in Salt Lake City. Despite growing up on a small rural farm, my parents felt it important for us to be exposed to culture and the arts. Most of the events we attended were free, but culture building none-the-less. As I sat in that old building the stain glass windows impressed me. In the light of the sunset, with the music as a backdrop, the windows came alive for me. My attention turned to the program for enough time that when I recalled the windows again and looked back, the sun had set and the window were dark and dull. I thought how love and kindness shining through us makes us bright, happy, and attractive like the sun shining through those windows, but if that light goes dim and we become selfish and self-centered our countenance is dulled. I do not now recall the exact analogy I made as a teenager looking out a darkened stained glass window, but I remember the elation I felt as the comparison came alive for me. I had done it. I found a life lesson in a mundane ordinary everyday thing.

I continue to draw life lessons from everyday events. That is the majority of what you will read in this book; life events, underscored by lessons learned. I encourage others to look for the encouraging in the mundane. Like your own modern parables, it makes for more interesting speeches and books. And it makes for a more rich and meaningful life.
</ManuscriptBlock>

My great grandfather’s father immigrated to the United States from Australia in the late 1800s. His father had migrated to Australia from England in an era when most people from England associated Australia with criminals. I don’t yet know the reason for his voyage but I know that my grandfather and his father were tough men. During the great depression of the 1930s, when despair and tragedy crippled the nation, my great-grandfather, Charles Sparrow, was not selling, he was buying. He acquired a significant stretch of ground in an arid valley in South Eastern Idaho; which he cleared of sagebrush and rocks with the help of good horses and four sons.  In the 1940s, when his other brothers received agricultural deferments to stay out of the war, my grandfather, Raymond Sparrow, enlisted in the infantry. Privet Sparrow was in France guarding prisoners of war when, in an escape attempt, he was shanked in the abdomen with a jagged piece of metal. While recovering aboard a hospital ship, it was discovered that he could weld and so he spent the rest of the war welding ships. He returned home with little fanfare; to a family farm where brothers had already staked claims. Rather than cause contention, he sought employment elsewhere. There were plenty of infrastructure projects in those days and his skills as a welder would serve him well. My aunt, in later years, presented me with his Eisenhower jacket; which I consider one of my prizest possessions. I never asked Grandpa Sparrow about his time in WWII. I never asked him much about anything, despite working next to him almost daily on the farm. What I relayed I know only from the little my dad told me.

I pause from story-telling at this point to impart what little wisdom I have, as I will do throughout the book. The first “to do” is find out who you are, and where you came from. I know it’s not earth shattering advice and most readers will determine right now whether to read on or add this to the stack of books they started to read but never finished. Also already obvious… I have some learning to do myself; as I don’t know why my third-great-grandfather went to Australia in the first place. But truth is true regardless of how simple. And the power of self-reflection does not cease to amaze me. Knowing who you are helps establish a starting point. It provides a lens through which to look out into the future and determine where it is you want to be. Many self-help and leadership books start here; with creating a vision. I agree that knowing where you are going is invaluable, but, I add, it starts with understanding where you are. And where you are starts with knowing where you came from. The first lesson, spoiler alert, is tied inseparably to the last. If you understand to power of the past you also understand the power you have on the future. Leaving a legacy and …..

Many cultures understand their powerful connection to their ancestors

Spirit of Elijah Malachi 4:5-6 he shall turn the heart of the fathers to the children and the heart of the children to the fathers”.

(Flow, 4%, and Daniel Pink)
Steven Kotler, in The Rise of Superman, describes flow as living in that narrow channel between boredom and anxiety—and he shares a wonderfully specific rule of thumb: flow tends to show up when the challenge is about 4% beyond your current skill level. Not 40%. Four. Stretch without panic.
Daniel Pink’s Drive explains why that feels so good: when people have autonomy, mastery, and purpose, motivation stops feeling like force and starts feeling like momentum.
Preschool is secretly perfect for this. The stakes are low, the curiosity is high, and everything is just a little bit new. You’re working hard—without feeling like you’re “working.”
That’s the goal in grown-up life too.

This has become core to everything I’ve tried to become, to whatever degree of success or failure, as a leader and as a person.
I was a pretty awkward kid (hold for gasps from the audience). I know, I know, I’m a pretty awkward adult too. But in spite of the struggles and anxiety it has caused in my life at times, on the whole I’m grateful for the lessons I’ve learned from being awkward and self-conscious.
The biggest one has been that no matter how weird I feel in a given situation, if I shift the focus from myself to others it always goes better.
Once I’m focused on the person in front of me, and trying to understand them, I almost always like them. Once it’s clear to them that I genuinely care about them and what they’re saying, something magic happens. They lean in.
Like all of our lessons in this chapter, this idea is embarrassingly simple.
If you pay attention to the person in front of you instead of yourself, you’ll begin to understand them.
 If you understand them, you’ll probably care about them.
 If you care about them, you’ll treat them the way they want to be treated.
As simple as all of that is, are you doing it? As often as you should?
Me neither, let’s work on it together.
In The Charisma Myth, Olivia Fox Cabane breaks down the research around charisma to three things: Power, Presence, and Warmth.
If you’re with someone, pay attention to them, be present with them, try to understand them. As you understand them, you’ll care more about them. That creates warmth. All of this pulls you outside of yourself, making it easier to find confidence and power.
This is obviously oversimplified, but simple triggers that lead to healthier mindsets are what it’s all about. Sometimes the best we can be for now is a goldfish wearing a shark fin, or a sloth in a hustle t-shirt, and that’s a start!
So being kind, working hard and enjoying it, not wasting people’s time, and listening aren’t just nice things to do. They are critical to life, work, and even to your internal world. They’re worth pursuing and remembering to practice, for their own merits and for the good they lead you to.
Sometimes leadership is not brilliance.
Sometimes it is not strategy.
Sometimes it is not perfectly engineered incentives or a clearly articulated mission statement.
Sometimes it is just this:
Look at the person in front of you.
 Care about what they’re saying.
And
Just listen.
</ManuscriptBlock>

<ManuscriptBlock
  id="homer-values-definition-and-relative-worth"
  title="Values: Definition and Relative Worth"
  type="leadership-principle"
  voice="homer"
  status="baseline"
  chapter="chapter-two-values"
  tags={["values", "leadership"]}
  source="Leadership My Story 24MAR19.docx"
>
Grade school yielded to middle school and with middle school and with high school came the forging of the things that were important to me, the things that I valued.

Value denotes worth. The more valuable an item is, the more goods or services for which it may be exchanged. Value is always relative.  The price (amount of goods, services, or money) a person is willing to pay for something varies greatly with individuals. It also varies by supply and demand. The most valuable thing on earth is not gold, diamonds, or any other precious metal or gem. It is the substance of life, water. A human can live for several weeks without food. But after only a few days one will parish for lack of water. I suppose that if the air we breathe were to become more scarce i.e. the supply of air was limited, that air would pass water as the most valuable resource on earth, as one can live mere minutes without air. In the case of air the supply is out pacing the demand. Thank goodness.

A value may also be defined as a principal or quality, such as integrity or loyalty.  Such value(s) hold intrinsic value/worth. While the price of one’s integrity may be measured by the monetary compensation one is willing to exchange for that integrity, it is arguable that if an individual is willing to compromise their integrity for any price then they have no integrity in the first place. If someone claims to be loyal to a spouse, but “sells out” for someone more attractive, then where they truly loyal? I’m not suggesting that a person who escapes an abusive relationship is not a loyal person or values loyalty I am simply suggesting that something else was more valuable to them, such as life. It may even be that the loyalty to their children motivated them to leave. It may happen that a person values loyalty to a spouse to such an extent that they remain in an abusive relationship because of it. I don’t want to argue which value or set of values is the most important; only to point out that values vary by individual. Someone’s loyalty to a company may go only as far as that person is not offered more money by another company. But that same person may have enough loyalty not to sell that company’s secrets for any price.

Values also hold relative value. While I may generally esteem efficiency as an uncompromising value, if it takes me longer to achieve something to accommodate someone else or teach someone how to do it, the long term benefit may warrant the time “wasted”.

Standards must be clearly written and disseminated to all. Failure to meet standards must be dealt with in a swift and severe way. However, individuals who make mistakes and work hard to correct and reduce mistakes should be forgiven. This creates an atmosphere of honesty. When mistakes are made, which will happen, it is important to have a climate where it can be confessed wholly and honestly. We as leaders must be willing to admit our short comings and mistakes as well.

Forgiving subordinates fosters forgiveness from them when we make mistakes, which we will do.
