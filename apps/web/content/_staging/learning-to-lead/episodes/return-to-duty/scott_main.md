**Week 39 - Oct 14: Return to Duty**  
_Theme: Resilience vs. Injury (Melting the Husky)_  
[Chapter Nine: Part 3]  
Hours later with the hull still smoldering and the adrenaline warn off, I called over the radio for an update on Privet Hanson. “Battle Main, Wolf, over”  
“Wolf, Battle Main”  
“Looking for an update on my Soldier who was MEDEVACed, over”  
“Wait one,” I guessed they wouldn’t know just how bad he was but would a least be able to tell me he was on his way to Germany or the States or something.  
“Wolf, Battle Main”  
“Battle Main, Wolf send it”  
“He’s RTD, over”  
“Say again, over”  
“I say again, That Soldier is returned to duty” I knew what it meant; it just didn’t make sense.  
I hesitated, “Battle Main this is Wolf, from the route clearance team. We had a Soldier hit by a huge IED that was MEDEVACed a couple hours ago. I highly doubt he will return to duty any time soon.”  
After a long pause on the net, for which I figured he would get his facts straight, he replied “Wolf, I have battle roster number K1134”  
“That’s him, over”  
“RTD, Wolf, over”  
I couldn’t believe it  
The Husky PVT Hansen operated could not be recovered. The fire burned for hours, so hot that many parts melted to the ground. When a team of engineers was sent to extract the hull. A Bradly was struck by another giant IED killing two Soldiers, leaving the hull on an island. With the blast hole from the IED that hit the Husky separating the road in front of it and the blast hole from the IED that hit the Bradly severing the road on the other side. My good buddy LT Pete forever gave me a hard time for melting down the best Husky in the fleet, that we borrowed from him, to a pile of molten metal  
It would be less than a week later that we got PVT Hansen back behind the wheel of a Husky. As we traversed down route gnat, PVT Hansen’s vehicle was struck again by an IED. It was a relatively small explosion. PVT Hansen almost immediately got on the radio and reported “I’m ok” in a discussed voice that in just two words described how painful to was to be blown up again. After a deliberate search by the team for potential booby traps, SSG Heddlesten climbed onto the Husky and opened the hatch to check on Hansen.  
That night I finally allowed myself to pause and reflect on the events of the last few weeks. I couldn’t help but be proud of the misfit team. We had come a long way from the fights over whether we should rush to save or deliberately look for secondaries.  
I have learned many things, many the hard way, by life experience and failure. The critical thing is not how learning happens but that it does. Writing our lessons down is not just helpful for some distant descendant that may read our words. It is most helpful for you! Making mistakes is human and part of life. Making the same mistakes over again is crazy by definition.  
Sometimes you don’t realize how far you’ve come until you get to the end of the journey and look back at the road you traveled.
