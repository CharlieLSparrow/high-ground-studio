**Charlie Sidebars**
   

**Character**
 
(Identity, trust, and the dog nobody needed
 
The most powerful lesson Dad ever taught me is this:
 
“It’s just what we do.”
 
It doesn’t sound like much at first. There’s no speech attached to it, no big defining moment. It just sort of… sits there.
 
And then, over time, it starts explaining things.
 
I’ve thought about that drive to Winnemucca more than I expected. Not in big reflective moments, but in the middle of normal life. Driving somewhere. Doing dishes. Something small happens, and the thought just shows up:
 
Oh right… he actually did that.
 
Fourteen hours. For a dog who, in strictly farm terms, was more of a lifestyle decision than a contributor.
 
No offense to Tip, my dog, who was a wonderful addition to the family and a questionable addition to the farm. Tip—like Scott once with a flashlight, and me most days—was enthusiastic… but not especially useful.
 
But that’s the part that makes the story stick.
 
Not the effort.
 
The ease of it.
 
For him, it wasn’t a story. It was just what you do when you’ve said you’ll do something.
 
In Atomic Habits, James Clear writes, “Every action you take is a vote for the type of person you wish to become.” What I didn’t understand at the time is that Dad had already been voting for a long time. That trip wasn’t a decision so much as the natural outcome of a pattern.
 
And now, thirty years later, I still think about it. Not constantly. Just often enough to notice that it stayed.
 
What felt monumental to me was, for him, a small expression of something much larger.
 
For a man whose life is built on acts of kindness and mercy, that drive wasn’t exceptional.
 
It was a drop in the bucket.
 
That’s the part that lingers.
 
Because it reframes character in a way that’s both encouraging and a little inconvenient. It suggests that character isn’t built in the moments we’d like it to be. It’s built in the small ones… the ones that don’t feel like anything at the time.
 
Until they do.
 
And eventually, those moments stack into something people can rely on.
 
Not because you said the right thing.
 
Because you keep doing it.
 
And after a while, people stop wondering who you’re going to be.
 
They already know.
    
**Standards + Forgiveness**
 
(Patterns, honesty, and whole-tank-of-milk mistakes)
 
There is a very specific kind of silence that follows a mistake you cannot undo.
 
You look at what just happened, and there isn’t a version of the story where you come out looking especially capable.
 
This was that.
 
A whole tank of milk is gone.
 
Not misplaced.  
Not reduced.
 
Gone in a way that suggests you may need to reconsider your relationship with responsibility.
 
And in that moment, the brain doesn’t reach for nuance. It doesn’t say, “Let’s separate this event from long-term identity.” It says something closer to,
 
Well… I guess this is who I am now.
 
Which is why I’ve always liked the line from Moneyball:
 
“It’s a process. It’s a process. It’s a process.”
 
It sounds dismissive until you realize it’s protective.
 
One moment is noise.  
A pattern is signal.
 
In Black Box Thinking, Matthew Syed describes how aviation treats mistakes. They log them, study them, share them. Because the goal isn’t to pretend mistakes don’t happen, but to make sure they don’t happen the same way twice.
 
Medicine, historically, hasn’t always worked that way.
 
Same humans. Different response.
 
“A closed culture of blame prevents learning. An open culture allows it.”
 
That line does a lot of work.
 
Because once mistakes are hidden, they grow.  
Once they’re named, they shrink.
 
That’s the line Scott is drawing.
 
A mistake is an event.  
A pattern is a trajectory.
 
They made a mistake. They were not the mistake.
 
And that distinction is what made it possible to tell the truth in the first place.
 
Which is the whole game.
 
Because if people can’t tell you what happened, you can’t fix what’s happening.
 
Or, to stay in baseball for just a moment, you don’t bench a player for one bad swing.
 
You start paying attention if he keeps missing the same pitch.
    
**Attention + Time**
 
(Focus, usefulness, and holding the light)
 
I like the flashlight story because it’s funny… and a little uncomfortable.
 
Scott is up top, telling a story. Dad is down in a frozen hole, trying to fix something important. And the flashlight is… doing its best.
 
Which feels about right.
 
Not because we’ve all stood over a well in Idaho winter, but because we’ve all been in that posture. Close to the work. Caring about the work. Just slightly misaligned with what’s actually needed.
 
We’re near it.  
We’re engaged.
 
We’re just not quite helping.
 
That’s why this always makes me think of Sherlock and Deep Work. Sherlock’s advantage isn’t just intelligence. It’s attention. He notices what matters and filters out what doesn’t. Newport says the same thing more quietly: focus is rare… because distraction is everywhere.
 
Attention isn’t just a preference.
 
It’s a resource.
 
And like most resources, it doesn’t like being split.
 
What we call multitasking is mostly just doing two things a little worse with a lot of confidence. Which means Scott wasn’t failing because he didn’t care.
 
He cared in two directions at once.
 
Talking felt like helping.
 
It just wasn’t.
 
The job was simple:
 
Hold the light.
 
Steady.  
Where it’s needed.
 
And that turns out to be a bigger idea than it sounds.
 
Because a surprising amount of life looks exactly like that. Not dramatic. Not complicated. Just being present enough, steady enough, and useful enough for someone else to do something difficult.
 
And over time, that kind of usefulness compounds.
 
So does the opposite.
 
Which is where habits really form.
 
Not in big decisions.
 
In where your attention goes… again, and again.
 
So maybe the lesson isn’t just “pay attention.”
 
Maybe it’s this:
 
Hold the light.
    
**Warmth + Correction**
 
(Watson, presence, and not loud… precise)
 
I’ve spent an unreasonable amount of time enjoying Sherlock Holmes stories.
 
At some point, it occurred to me that I wasn’t just enjoying Sherlock.
 
I was enjoying Watson.
 
The dependable one. The one who stays.
 
And that realization shifted something.
 
Because I had quietly assumed that leadership looked like Gandalf. Or Yoda. Calm, wise, a few perfectly timed sentences and everything falls into place.
 
It turns out… that’s not me.
 
And more importantly, it’s not always what people need.
 
What people need is someone who shows up. Who listens. Who stays steady when things get a little sideways.
 
Watson.  
Samwise.  
R2-D2.
 
Not the hero.  
Not the sage.
 
The one who is there.
 
Which is why the parking lot moment works.
 
Dad doesn’t give a speech. He doesn’t escalate. He simply says, “Your mom is worried about you.”
 
Not loud… precise.
 
It lands, not because the sentence is extraordinary, but because everything behind it is.
 
The relationship.  
The trust.  
The consistency.
 
Once I stopped trying to be impressive, it got a lot easier to be useful.
 
And that’s a much better place to live.
 
Because the goal was never really to be interesting.
 
Just to be interested.
 
And when it matters…
 
be the one who stays.  
be the one who listens.  
be the one who helps.
 
Be the one who holds the light.
