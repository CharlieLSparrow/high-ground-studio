Brief history of Iraq
 
Brief history of Route Clearance
 
Ramadi
 
==At the start of the new Millenium in 2000, Ramadi was a Sunni, tribal city along the Euphrates River. Under Saddam Hussein, order was enforced through the state, not through local trust. It was not super prosperous, but it was predictable.==
 
==That predictability vanished after 2003. With the fall of the regime, security forces collapsed and Ramadi became a center of the insurgency. Roads were seeded with improvised explosive devices. Small arms fire and ambushes were routine. Movement through the city was slow, dangerous, and deliberate. By 2006, Ramadi was widely considered one of the most violent cities in Iraq many considered Ramadi to be the most dangerous city in the world at that time.==
 
==The fighting that followed is referred to as the Battle of Ramadi. It was not one single engagement, but more like a prolonged, grinding campaign to regain control of the city. Units including the Ready First Brigade, Task Force Bruiser, and the unit I serve with, the 321st Engineer Battalion operated in and around Ramadi during this period. Combat outposts were established inside neighborhoods, patrols pushed into areas long controlled by insurgents, and route clearance operations ran night after night in a strategy called clear, hold , build. Progress was measured in blocks, not miles.==
 
==By late 2007, the situation changed dramatically. Violence dropped, aided by the Anbar Awakening and sustained coalition presence. The contrast was stark. Streets that had recently been littered with improvised explosive devices became safe enough for daily life. Soldiers who once never left their vehicles without full body armor were suddenly running neighborhood 5K races in exercise shorts. Markets reopened, traffic returned, and for a time, Ramadi felt stable. Not perfect, but normal enough to be believable.==
 
==That stability did not last. After the U.S. withdrawal in 2011, political marginalization and weakening security created space for extremist groups to return. In 2015, the Islamic State captured Ramadi. What followed was destruction, instability, and uncertainty. Large portions of the city were devastated during the fight to retake it, which Iraqi forces completed in 2016.==
 
==As of this writing, Ramadi is quieter than it was in the mid-2000s or during the ISIS occupation. People live there again, and the city continues to rebuild. But its history is a reminder of how quickly conditions can shift, and how hard-won stability can be undone. Ramadi stands as both a warning and an example of what sustained effort can achieve, and how fragile those gains can be once that effort fades.==
