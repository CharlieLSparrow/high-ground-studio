**Samwise Clip**
 
As my siblings and I played in the barnyard on a summer day, the dry summer air seemed to blow in an unknown friendly dog from out of nowhere. She was a hyper pup with long hair, a long nose, and a perpetually happy face. She was no Sheppard, Collie, or Sheepdog of any kind. Clearly not a working dog at all, defiantly nothing like the red healer, Bandit, we had that could bring the cows in from the corral for milking with one “get ‘em”. She was clearly a stray, and clearly a mutt. But when my little brother played the puppy dog eyes on my dad, Dad agreed that we could run an ad in the paper and if no one claimed the thing, Charlie could keep it. When after weeks of asking around and running ads proved fruitless, the promise to keep the pup was honored and Dad gave in. A black end to her all white tail gave her the name Tip, or Tippie, as Aunt Dena Rae called her, and she became part of the family. She wasn’t much for herding cows, in fact Grandpa would often lock her in the granary when livestock needed to be herded, because she would run around and scatter the animals rather than direct them. Even with all the effort to show her what to do, she just wanted to play. But she couldn’t even play fetch, dumb mutt. It didn’t matter; my little brother loved her. aw
 
==*I really did! I loved that dumb dog so much. She was a kindred spirit loving the farm, full of enthusiasm, but not particularly useful.==
 
Then, one day, Tip was nowhere to be fund. She was not at the barn, not at grandma's house, not in the fields or the pasture, or at the neighbor down the dusty road. She had disappeared as suddenly as she had appeared. Kids cried and prayed. One night about a week after Tip was lost my little brother made an odd request. “Dad,” he pleaded with the same puppy eyes he used to keep the dog in the first place, “will you go get Tippie no matter how far away she is.” “Of course,” the answer came back quickly. For surly, how far away could the little dog be.
 
==*It's weird that I made a request that specific. I knew he could be trusted “no matter how far” he had to go. **Leaders who are trusted are leaders who are followed.**

**Nate Clip**
 
We had a small plot of 20 acres behind the house and my grandpa had 40 acres behind his place. With the 30 acres we leased from the neighbors, it kept a boy with some work to do in the summer. My dad worked for the West Cache Canal Company bringing water across the high Southern Idaho step desert to the thirsty sandy fields. It was a bit unpredictable where he might be on a given day but he was always predictably busy through the summer months. In addition, the milk from the dairy was our family’s livelihood. Dad used to say things like, “cow’s don’t care if you’re busy or tired.” “They don’t care if it’s Saturday or a holiday or your birthday.” ”They have to be milked, twice a day, every day.” “There are no days off”. On his busy days my dad would load our bikes in the back of the old white pickup truck and drop my sister and I off at the barn. With the cows milked we would ride the mile and a half back home. At the end of the day, if it didn’t look like dad would make it home for milking, we would ride the bikes back down to the barn to milk the cows again. My dad trusted my sister and me with incredible responsibility for pre-teen adolescence. For the most part we made him proud, but we did have our moments. When we cleaned out the pipes after milking we removed the line from the tank to flush a rinse cycle, which we fed to the calves, and then ran the cleaning solution through. At the beginning of each milking the line would be reconnected to the tank. I remember one evening when the milking was entirely completed, I went into the wash room to remove the line from the tank. The line was already removed and I knew immediately by the milk splashed around the drain that it had not been recently removed by my sister. We had literally dumped the entire milking down the drain. My dad was rightfully upset when we called him over the CB radio to let him know what had happened. "Base 2 to Mobile 2". "Base 2 this is Mobile 2, go ahead" "Dad, we forgot to connect the pipe to the tank when we started milking tonight." Ok? "We didn't notice until we were done milking and went to wash up." Silence. "Dad, we dumped it all down the drain." The radio was quiet for a long time. I knew he was upset. "Thanks for letting me know." was all he said. I was surprised at how quickly we were forgiven and confidence was restored. It was clearly accidental. He truly trusted us and he needed us.

==So how do we decide when someone is worth keeping them when they've cost us dearly, and when do we let someone go?==
 
Leaders have a responsibility to remove form their organization individuals who have a habit of doing things that are not beneficial and/or detrimental to the organization. This must be done for two reasons. The first is obvious, replace the person who is not pulling their weight in order to put a more productive person in their stead. The second is may be less obvious, but actually more important. You have to cut out cancerous, bad strawberries from destroying the moral of the rest of the workers. It can be extremely de-motivating to see someone doing a poor or even just mediocre job who receives the same treatment, pay, rewards, etc. as someone who is working much harder. Keeping under performers around brings everyone to that low level of expectation. It is extremely difficult sometimes as a leader not to reward poor performance with indifference and to reward hard work with more work. Standards must be clearly written and disseminated to all. Failure to meet standards must be dealt with in a swift and severe way. However, individuals who make mistakes and work hard to correct and reduce mistakes should be forgiven. This creates an atmosphere of honesty. When mistakes are made, which will happen, it is important to have a climate where it can be confessed wholly and honestly. We as leaders must be willing to admit our own short comings and mistakes as well. Forgiving subordinates fosters forgiveness from them when we make mistakes, which we will do. **Forgive and Fire, both are critical.**
 
**Roy Firing**
 
==One of our favorite leadership books is== _The Dichotomy of Leadership_ ==in it Jocko Willink shares a poignant anecdote about a SEAL officer who was a "great guy", personally liked by the team, hardworking, and deeply committed. But he consistently struggled with situational awareness and tactical decision-making.==
 
==They spent weeks mentoring him, providing extra "reps," and offering direct feedback. They liked him, the team liked him, they== _wanted_ ==him to succeed, but he just couldn't perform at the level they needed and they had to pull him from the platoon.==
 
==If a leader allows a subpar performer to remain in a critical role out of "kindness," they are being cruel to the rest of the team who depend on everyone meeting the standard, and they're often being cruel to the underperformer by holding them captive in a role where they can't be successful.==
 
**"The leader is responsible for the performance of the team. If an individual is dragging the team down and cannot be coached up, the leader must make the hard call."**
 
==Personally I've never regrated letting someone go in retrospect because I believe it's been in the best interest of the mission and the person to do so. More often than not struggling team members have been able to progress to where they needed to be, in rare cases I've had to let people go, and in a couple situations I held on to people much longer than what was good for them or the organization and those are the situations I regret most deeply. I harmed both the people and the organization by holding them in roles they weren't suited for.==
 
==The initial default should focus on coaching, teaching, mentoring, and counseling. Turnover is costly in so many ways, not the least of which is the time and emotional efforts of reforming teams.==
 
==Team development inject==
 
==Efforts to retain should emphasize an individual's strengths and how they can be utilized effectively. Be conscious not to remove someone from your team simply because you don’t “get along”. Diversity is a valuable asset. Check yourself; are you dismissing this person because they disagree with you? Surrounding yourself with all “Yes” men and women, can be hurting the potential of the organization.==
 
==Diversity inject==
 
==On the other hand, as I said, I have unfortunately waited too long, on occasion, to let someone go, thinking I was helping the individual, thinking I was helping the team. I later recognized the responsibility of firing was mine alone, as the leader, and I had hurt the team by not taking charge of it. The good news is that if you have done the formal, written counseling and can demonstrate what expectations were and where the individual failed to meet those expectations, if you have clearly communicated the consequences of not meeting objectives, then the firing feels more logical and less threatening to both the one doing the firing and the one leaving. Removing someone from your team does not have to be painful or dramatic. Unless they have done something illegal or unethical it can simply be an opportunity for them to pursue options that are a better fit for their skills.==
