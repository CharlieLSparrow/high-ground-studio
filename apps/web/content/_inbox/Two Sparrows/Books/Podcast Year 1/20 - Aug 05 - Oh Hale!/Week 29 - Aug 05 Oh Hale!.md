**Week 29 - Aug 05: Oh Hale!**  
_Theme: SSG Hale: Decentralized Command_  
[Chapter Six: Part 6]  
Oh Hale!  
From my estimation we sat at maybe the most dangerous intersections in the city of Ramadi, the intersection of Route Spartan and Route Goat; maybe only surpassed in IED threat by the crossing just one block to the North at the intersection of Goat and Muje. The tankers dubbed the east to west running Route Spartan ”the bowling alley”. Varying from 50-100 meters wide, it looked more like a dirt strip of trash and spars weeds, than a road. On one end stood a, once bustling, train station but now a station only for weapons and munitions of the insurgents. On the other end nearly a mile away the tanks could over-watch and fire their 120mm smooth boar into unsuspecting destruction. Intersecting Route Spartan running to the north, Route Goat held a reputation for so many IEDs that even after the route clearance team cleared the road, the infantry and armor units avoided it at all cost, certain that there were always more IEDs buried out of sight.  
The lead vehicle inched slowly forward and I felt uneasy.  
"1, 6" I called on the radio. The lack of response didn't help my anxiety. "2-1, 2-6, over" I called again, "Wolf 2-1, this is Wolf 2-6, over." I called like a mother calling her child by their full first, middle, and last name that lets them know she means business. "What?" SSG Hale finally came back, not at all within radio protocol and crossing the threshold of disrespectful. "1-6" I called back calm but anxious "How about you let the buffalo come up there and dig on that spot." No response. "1-6, did you copy my last, over." "Look 6, we're kinda busy up here so if you can hang tight for one minute that would be great." That hadn’t help calm me. Now I was anxious and upset. I waited a few minutes and watched the lead truck inch forward again. I did not feel good about it "1,6, I'd feel a lot better if you let the buffalo come up there and dig, over" "And I'd feel a lot better if you left me alone and let me do my job, over" He no longer flirted with insubordination, he stepped all over it. The situation tense already from an almost inevitable attack, was now at a boiling point with my squad leader, who appeared to be rogue, inching toward what I felt was certain doom. I picked up the mic to key the radio again when SSG Hale beat me to it. He keyed his mike. "Sir, if you don't trust me to do my job, then fire me. Otherwise let me do my job, Over" That was exactly what was about to happen. Finally, Hale had given me the definitive reason I needed to fire him. I was a wreck with emotions. Despite the daily danger, I didn't typically get very nervous about clearing IEDs. We left the wire and faced the possibility of injury every day, every day, every day. People asked me later how I dealt with that prospect so frequently. My response I find similar to the Incredible Hulk who's secret to staying calm is that he's always angry. I dealt with the prospect of dying by considering myself to already be dead. It helped me to focus on what I could do and not what I couldn’t control. But with the hair standing up on the back of my neck and the churning intensifying in my gut with every inch the lead truck nosed farther North on Route Goat I couldn't ignore the desire to do something.  
"If you don't trust me to do my job, then fire me." I mulled that over in my head. "I will fire you." I thought. Did I trust him? I questioned myself. How was I perceived by the rest of the platoon right now? I wondered. I must seem weak and not in control. My subordinate had been insubordinate and I should quash that right now, right? If I don't, what message am I sending? I don't know if I can trust him. What can I trust. I did trust SPC Orton the spotter and gunner but was he persuasive enough to direct a good outcome. I also explicitly trusted SPC Brown, the driver, and he held his foot on the accelerator and the break peddle. I did trust that team. And for that, I risked the back lash it might cause and kept my mouth shut.  
After what seemed like an eternity, Hale finally called again on the radio as the lead truck moved back out of the way. "Ok, 6, send the buffalo up to dig right there." A green laser beamed from his window and drew a line in the dirt. The Buffalo moved forward the robotic arm extended and poked around a bit. "6,1, we spent a good amount of time up there analyzing and we concur that the best chance of finding an IED is to dig right there, over." Again the green laser drew a line in the road. The arm operator maneuvered and dug a few inches to the right then a few inches to the left, the as the green laser danced emphatically over the exact spot the team wanted searched. A pass directly along the line of the laser revealed the prize, several projectiles bundled together and configured to destroy an armored vehicle and kill its occupants. The remainder of the missions was no less exciting with the lead team finding a total of 7 IEDs and 1 Unexploded Ordnance (UXO) (basically an IED that had not yet been wired as a booby trap) before an IED went off on the Explosive Ordnance Disposal (EOD) tech, peppering him with shrapnel in his back side ending further interrogation. It was a lot to digest, not the least of which involved the looming conversation to be had about the way SSG Hale addressed the platoon leader at a tense moment.  
I did trust him and his team but the trust had to be mutual, he had to trust me as well. We worked out our procedures over the next few days and he kept his job.  
Trust  
I had a platoon of misfits. SSG Hale used to say that he trusted them with my life but wouldn’t trust them to wash his car. He was serious about both.  
One other note. Don't let your ego get in the way of having people on your team that challenge you. Surrounding yourself with ”yes” men doesn’t lead to solutions to problems you haven't thought of. Respect is key and someone who doesn’t always agree, is not the same as someone who is disagreeable.
