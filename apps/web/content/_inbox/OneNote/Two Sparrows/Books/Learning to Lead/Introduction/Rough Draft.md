**Introduction**  
On Sundays at my house, growing up in Southeast Idaho, my mom, a small, thin woman with light skin but dark eyes and short dark hair, would get out of bed at 5:00 a.m. (sleeping in, compared to the rest of the week for her). At that hour she prepared roast and potatoes for Sunday dinner - not a big surprise when you have dairy cows and potato fields, but a luxury we were not allowed to take for granted. After three hours of church, and before the cows had to be milked again, I would sit around the dark wood table on big wooden chairs with my two sisters, my brother, and mom. My dad at the head of the table would ask each of us, in turn, what we learned at church that day. It was at those times that he often attempted to impart words of fatherly wisdom to his progeny.  
One day as we sat around that table in the house on bend in the road, with the rust colored linoleum beneath our feet, he told of a small swallow he saw earlier that week ferociously defending her nest from a red-tailed hawk. I stared out the big dining room window across the field with hay laying in neat rows. I lost track of the story as I watched birds of all sizes discovering rodents, bugs, and other feasts newly exposed by the freshly cut crop. His story complete, he paused. The quiet pulled me from my daydream. I had missed the point of the tale. I suppose the moral of that particular story was less important than what he shared next. He looked passed me sitting at the foot of the table across from him, passed the golden Formica countertops behind me, passed the classic gold refrigerator of that late 70s home, through the door, through the carport, and out into the pastures beyond. Then he encouraged us all to look for life lessons in everyday things. It seemed pretty simple for such dramatic effect. Intrigued, and always looking to please or impress my father.  
I watched for months but saw nothing. I tried to come up with things at random… This banana peel is like when you… get… old… or… brown spots… that… or… even throw it away... I got nothing. I figured I just didn’t see things the way my dad did. Discouraged, I almost gave up entirely. Then one night I went to see a symphony and concert at the Assembly Hall in Salt Lake City. Despite growing up on a small rural farm, my parents felt it important for us to be exposed to culture and the arts. Most of the events we attended were free, but culture building none-the-less. As I sat in that old historic building the stain glass windows impressed me. In the light of the sunset, with the music as a backdrop, the windows came alive for me. My attention turned to the program for enough time that when I recalled the windows again and looked back, the sun had set and the window were dark and dull. I thought how love and kindness shining through us makes us bright, happy, and attractive like the sun shining through those windows, but if that light goes dim, and we become selfish and self-centered, our countenance is dulled. In honesty, I do not now recall the exact analogy I made as a teenager looking out a darkened stained glass window, but I remember the elation I felt as the comparison came alive for me. I had done it. I found a life lesson in a mundane, ordinary, everyday thing.  
I continue to draw life lessons from everyday events. That is the majority of what you will read in this book; life events, underscored by lessons learned, sometimes validated by other authors and professionals.  
Like Aesop and his famous fables, I feel compelled to point out the moral I extrapolate from these stories and experiences. There are, of course, innumerable potential parallels and lessons to be learned. Please do not constrain yourself by my limited experience and imagination. The point and intent is that these events might inspire, encourage, motivate, or resonate in some way, whatever that is for you. I also encourage readers to ==look for the encouraging, enlightening lessons in the challenging and mundane of your own lives==. Like your own modern parables, it makes for more interesting speeches and books. And it makes for a more rich and meaningful life.
 
### **Charlie's Note: Your Brain on Stories**

It turns out Dad’s advice to “look for life lessons in everyday things” was a masterclass in effective teaching and leadership. He was tapping into the core of how we learn and process the world around us, our brains are fundamentally wired to prioritize stories over statistics. These stories can often have a much deeper impact than we would ever expect.  
For example, there was this group of researchers at BYU who, trying to find an antidote to the rising tide of teen anxiety and low self-esteem, decided to test an old idea: that knowing your roots keeps you grounded. They surveyed hundreds of young adults, asking them detailed questions about the lives of their parents and grandparents. The results were stunning. The more the students knew about their family's stories—the triumphs, the struggles, the meaningful anecdotes—the healthier their sense of identity was. A later BYU study found that participating in family history research can increase self-esteem by 8% and reduce anxiety by a staggering 20%. Our family stories become our anchors, reminding us that we’re part of a larger narrative.³  
But why are stories so powerful? Neuroscientist Paul Zak set out to answer that exact question. In his lab, he showed people a short, emotional video about a father and his two-year-old son, Ben, who has terminal brain cancer. He then showed a control group a "flat" version of the story, the same father and son on a pleasant day at the zoo. By drawing blood before and after, Zak made a remarkable discovery. The emotional, character-driven story caused participants' brains to release oxytocin, a neurochemical he dubbed the "moral molecule". This is the same chemical that bonds mothers to their babies, and it generated intense feelings of empathy for the characters. This empathy wasn't just a feeling; it translated directly into action. After the video, participants who had the oxytocin spike were significantly more likely to donate money to a stranger. A good story changes our brain chemistry, making us more trusting, more empathetic, and more likely to act.  
This is also how Procter & Gamble statistician Jim Bangel became a minor corporate celebrity. Tasked with writing dry, data filled monthly memos, he decided one day to wrap his statistics in a story about a character named "Earnest Engineer". Soon, his reports, featuring a growing cast that included "Max Profit" and "Sella Case," were being requested by employees all over the company. He didn't change the data; he just gave it a soul. If I can end on this very cheesy but true adage: spreadsheets inform, but stories transform.
