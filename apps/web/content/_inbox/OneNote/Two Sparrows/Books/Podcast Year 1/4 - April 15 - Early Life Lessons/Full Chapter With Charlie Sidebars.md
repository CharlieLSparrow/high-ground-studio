# CHAPTER ONE

# The Early Days
 
## 🎵 Preschool Memories

My earliest memories are of school. The earliest is of preschool, running around in circles. I remember the song we sang about it:  
🎶  
“See me run,  
running around in circles,  
running and running,  
running around the room”.  
🎶  
Songs are a powerful way to remember things. Vivid images, smells, and emotions also connect those synapses in the brain in binding ways that make them forever easy to recall.  
I remember in pre-school a kid named Cody eating my cousin Andy’s glue; that evokes some vivid images, smells, and emotions.  
I learned a lot from Mrs. Kathy at Kerr Preschool. I learned and worked hard, but I didn’t know that I was learning or working hard. I simply enjoyed it.
 
### 📌 Lesson learned:

**work hard and enjoy it.**   That is a theme comes up again and again in my life.  
I have not ever been the smartest, strongest, most attractive, or most social person in the ”office“, but being a person that others can count on, because they know you will contribute meaningful effort, makes me a valuable member of the team.  
For the other part, having fun does not have to mean implementing recess and playing on the merry-go-round at lunch (although it doesn’t need to be ruled out either). It does mean that the work environment should be enjoyable enough for workers not to prefer to get in a car accident accompanied by a trip to the hospital on the way to work in order to avoid setting foot in the building. (Please tell me I’m not the only one to have felt that way before.)  
It doesn’t require giggling pillow fights or slides between floors to be fun, although I love that idea (The giggling fights not the silly slide idea, of course). It does require some recognition of humanity, which is possible in all organizations, yes, including serious and dangerous organizations such as the military.
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Effort + Joy

_(Flow, 4%, and Daniel Pink)_  
Steven Kotler, in _The Rise of Superman_, describes flow as living in that narrow channel between boredom and anxiety—and he shares a wonderfully specific rule of thumb: flow tends to show up when the challenge is about **4% beyond your current skill level**. Not 40%. Four. Stretch without panic.  
Daniel Pink’s _Drive_ explains why that feels so good: when people have **autonomy, mastery, and purpose**, motivation stops feeling like force and starts feeling like momentum.  
Preschool is secretly perfect for this. The stakes are low, the curiosity is high, and everything is just a little bit new. You’re working hard—without feeling like you’re “working.”  
That’s the goal in grown-up life too.
 
# 🏫 First Grade

I don’t remember much about the first grade, only that my teacher Mrs. Terman quickly became known as Mrs. “Termite”; which is hilarious when you’re a six year old boy. Being the recipient of such ridicule is, however, less humorous.  
Called names, or told, “Scotty, Watty, needs to go Potty”, sounds ridiculous now, but devastated my six year old self.
 
### 📌 Lesson learned:

**Be kind.**  
No one likes to be called names or belittled.  
It seems like a simple lesson but apparently, many leaders did not learn in the first grade that bullying and hazing along with, sexist, racial, comments, ethnic slurs and innuendos are not appropriate.  
Don’t do it. Don’t laugh at it. Don’t tolerate it. Even if it is sarcastic or in jest, it is not ok, it’s not funny, stop it!
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Psychological Safety

_(Project Aristotle & Amy Edmondson)_  
Google’s 2012 “Project Aristotle” studied over 180 teams and found that the strongest predictor of performance was not intelligence or experience.  
It was **psychological safety**, a term developed by Harvard professor Amy Edmondson describing an environment where people feel safe to speak without fear of humiliation.  
First-grade ridicule shrinks contribution.  
Kindness is not sentimental.  
It is structural.  
Teams do not innovate while bracing for impact.
 
# 😴 Second Grade

My second grade teacher attempted quiet time; which worked only because if we were quiet enough, for long enough, she would fall asleep, and if we stayed quiet, she would stay asleep.  
Even 7 year old boys can be quite if it means getting away with mischief.  
There was always one sweet girl who would burst the bubble of dream time by waking the teacher, to everyone else’s dismay.
 
### 📌 Lesson learned:

**never trust sweet girls…**  
That’s probably not it.  
I’m probably just upset because I totally had a crush on her and I never said anything.  
Maybe there is a better lesson here for me or someone else to glean; then again, it may be true that “sometimes a cigaret butt is just a cigaret butt"(attributed to Sherlock Holmes) in other words, maybe sometimes a funny story is just that, an entertaining, funny story.  
Or, maybe I will understand this story better by the time I get to chapter 4.5.
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Incentives

_(Freakonomics & Behavioral Design)_  
_Freakonomics_ popularized a basic truth: behavior is often shaped more by incentives than by speeches.  
Quiet time worked because the reward structure worked. The boys weren’t obedient, they were strategic.  
Leadership tends to overuse commands and underuse design. If you want behavior to change, you don’t just lecture it. You engineer the environment around it.  
And yes, sometimes a funny story is just a funny story, but it's always worth considering what behaviour you're actually encouraging.
 
What is your system actually incentivizing?
 
# 📝 Third Grade

I discovered something about myself in the third grade that I didn’t understand well until many years later and I was not able to have a handle on it until years after that.  
I learned that I didn’t like busy work.  
I discovered, at the age of 8, that sometimes my teacher, Mrs. Peterburg, gave us things to do for the sole purpose of keeping us busy and, by default, quiet.  
No one likes to have their time wasted; not even an 8 year olds.
 
### 📌 Lesson learned:

**Don’t waste people’s time, it is precious to them.**
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Autonomy & Purpose

_(Self-Determination Theory & Shackleton)_  
Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) says humans are motivated by autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Busy work violates autonomy. It quietly says: _your time isn’t valuable._  
Shackleton’s 1914 expedition is a sharp contrast. Those men endured horror because they understood the mission. Purpose didn’t remove hardship it made hardship survivable.  
People will do extraordinary things when the work matters. And they’ll resent ordinary things when it doesn’t.
 
# 💾 Seventh Grade

In that year I took a keyboarding class. In that class I discovered, and subsequently became a master of, the “copy and paste”, made possible by the advent of WordPerfect.  
I finished my work in a third of the time as my classmates, whom I thought were not nearly as smart as me.  
While they typed away, I played “Scorched Earth” off of a floppy disk I brought to class with me.  
I mastered sending a tiny blip across the screen at the right trajectory to hit the opposing player’s tiny pixels in the shape of a tank.  
I did not, however, master typing.  
By the time I realized the purpose for the repetitive typing of the same letters and sentences over and over, it was much later, and by later, I mean, too late.  
Even now as I type these words, looking at my fingers and pecking for the keys, I wish that I had understood the purpose of the task. I could have met the intent.
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Start With Why

_(Simon Sinek & Commander’s Intent)_  
Sinek’s _Start With Why_ hit because it named something we all feel: people can follow “what” and “how” but never actually buy in.  
One of my favorite thing's Homer has taught me from military principles is **Commander’s Intent.** When people understand intent behind the mission, they can adapt without micromanagement.  
Keyboarding class taught the task. It didn’t teach the intent.  
Without why, humans optimize for shortcuts. With why, they optimize for mastery.  
Again, what is your system actually incentivizing?  

# 🚲 The Farm Years

I worked hard on the farm, often times riding my bike to the barn a mile and a half away to milk the cows without supervision before my age had double digits.  
I did make up games to make long bike rides bearable.  
We had names for our bikes. Mine received the ingenious name “Predator”. Probably because it was written on the padding of the handlebars.  
I would call out the play by play of the ride as if I were an announcer at a horse race:  
“And Karen riding on Rainbow-Rider is lagging behind but here she comes with her second wind! She’s passing Schwinn! She’s coming around Husky! And it’s neck ’a neck with Predator! Here they come across the finish line! It will be a photo finish folks! Yes, it’s Rainbow-Rider over Predator by a tire width! Unbelievable! No one expected it! She’s making a victory lap waving to the crowd.”  
Games, singing songs, telling stories, or a good water fight, always made the hours go better.  
Yes cheesy, yes corny but true just the same as Mary Poppins sang, A spoon full of sugar helps the medicine go down. – Mary Poppins
 
### 📝 Charlie Sidebar: Meaning & Endurance

_(Viktor Frankl & Narrative Psychology)_  
In _Man’s Search for Meaning_, Viktor Frankl observed that those who could attach meaning to suffering endured it better.  
I almost hesitate to invoke Frankl at this point because I wouldn't ever compare our experiences to his, but as with many of the lessons I've learned from Homer's stories about combat, extremity has the tendency to strip away all but the most important lessons.  
And this is one of my favorite: Story transforms labor into purpose. Purpose brings meaning and sometimes even joy to difficulty.  
Naming the bike. Calling the race. Creating drama. It wasn't just distraction, it was resilience.
 
# 🔥 The Pillow Incident

When I was about ten years old my family stayed in a cabin with my Mom’s sister and her family for Thanksgiving.  
Stormy weather kept eight rowdy kids inside.  
No video games or toys in the cabin made a pillow war inevitable.  
Though not a regular “fight” it definitely escalated to a conflict of epic proportions.  
One fateful throw landed a pillow from a loft into the light fixture high above the floor.  
Smoke soon billowed from the super-heated pillow case.  
Panic followed, as the object was too high to reach.  
Children screamed and adults scrambled for things to stand on and objects long enough to reach the pillow before it burst into flames.  
In the chaos I calmly walked to the light switch and turned it off.  
Everyone froze, and the room went silent for a moment.  
The smoke began to clear.  
Then everyone broke into laughter with expressions of “I should have thought of that”.  
The truth is that, sometimes the solution to an issue within your organization is simple.  
Sometimes it comes from an unexpected person.  
Don’t be afraid to listen to everyone and don’t be afraid to suggest the simple solution you have either.
 
### Charlie Sidebar: Listening Culture

_(Challenger, Just Listen, and the light switch)_  
Before the Space Shuttle Challenger launched in 1986, engineers warned about O-ring failure in cold temperatures. Their concerns were not adequately elevated.  
Sometimes failure happens not because no one knew, but because no one was heard.  
Mark Goulston, in _Just Listen_, argues that people don’t need to be impressed first. They need to feel heard first. Listening lowers defenses, builds trust, and surfaces solutions that were already in the room.  
The light switch was always there.  
The question is whether someone is listening.
 
# Chapter Close: Awkwardness, Warmth, and Attention

This has become core to everything I’ve tried to become, to whatever degree of success or failure, as a leader and as a person.  
I was a pretty awkward kid (hold for gasps from the audience). I know, I know, I’m a pretty awkward adult too. But in spite of the struggles and anxiety it has caused in my life at times, on the whole I’m grateful for the lessons I’ve learned from being awkward and self-conscious.  
The biggest one has been that no matter how weird I feel in a given situation, if I shift the focus from myself to others it always goes better.  
Once I’m focused on the person in front of me, and trying to understand them, I almost always like them. Once it’s clear to them that I genuinely care about them and what they’re saying, something magic happens. They lean in.  
Like all of our lessons in this chapter, this idea is embarrassingly simple.  
If you pay attention to the person in front of you instead of yourself, you’ll begin to understand them.  
If you understand them, you’ll probably care about them.  
If you care about them, you’ll treat them the way they want to be treated.  
As simple as all of that is, are you doing it? As often as you should?  
Me neither, let’s work on it together.  
In _The Charisma Myth_, Olivia Fox Cabane breaks down the research around charisma to three things: Power, Presence, and Warmth.  
If you’re with someone, pay attention to them, be present with them, try to understand them. As you understand them, you’ll care more about them. That creates warmth. All of this pulls you outside of yourself, making it easier to find confidence and power.  
This is obviously oversimplified, but simple triggers that lead to healthier mindsets are what it’s all about. Sometimes the best we can be for now is a goldfish wearing a shark fin, or a sloth in a hustle t-shirt, and that’s a start!  
So being kind, working hard and enjoying it, not wasting people’s time, and listening aren’t just nice things to do. They are critical to life, work, and even to your internal world. They’re worth pursuing and remembering to practice, for their own merits and for the good they lead you to.  
Sometimes leadership is not brilliance.  
Sometimes it is not strategy.  
Sometimes it is not perfectly engineered incentives or a clearly articulated mission statement.  
Sometimes it is just this:  
Look at the person in front of you.  
Care about what they’re saying.  
And  
Just listen.
