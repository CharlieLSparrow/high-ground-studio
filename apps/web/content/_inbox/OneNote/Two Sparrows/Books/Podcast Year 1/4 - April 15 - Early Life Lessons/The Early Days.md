## The Early Days

My earliest memories are of school. The very earliest is preschool—running around in circles. I even remember the song:  
“See me run,  
running around in circles,  
running and running,  
running around the room.”  
Songs are powerful memory anchors. Vivid images, smells, and emotions bind synapses in the brain in ways that make them easy to recall forever.  
For example: I remember a kid named Cody eating my cousin Andy’s glue. That memory comes with vivid images, smells, and emotions.  
I learned a lot from Mrs. Kathy at Kerr Preschool. I worked hard—but I didn’t know I was working hard. I simply enjoyed it.
 
### **Lesson Learned:**

**Work hard—and enjoy it.**  
That theme comes up again and again in my life.  
I have never been the smartest, strongest, most attractive, or most social person in the “office.” But being someone others can count on—because they know you will contribute meaningful effort—makes you a valuable member of the team.  
Enjoyment doesn’t require recess or merry-go-rounds at lunch (though I’m not ruling that out). It means the environment shouldn’t make people secretly hope for a minor car accident on the way to work just to avoid walking through the doors. (Please tell me I’m not the only one who has felt that way.)  
It doesn’t require giggling pillow fights or slides between floors—although I do love that idea (the giggling fights, not the slides… mostly). It does require recognizing people’s humanity.  
And yes, that includes serious and dangerous organizations like the military.
 
## First Grade: Kindness Matters

I don’t remember much about first grade except that my teacher, Mrs. Terman, quickly became known as Mrs. “Termite.” Hilarious if you’re a six-year-old boy.  
Less hilarious if you’re the one being called names.  
“Scotty Watty needs to go Potty” sounds ridiculous now, but it devastated my six-year-old self.
 
### **Lesson Learned:**

**Be kind.**  
No one likes to be called names or belittled.  
It seems like a simple lesson, but apparently many leaders missed it in first grade. Bullying, hazing, sexist comments, racial slurs, ethnic innuendos—they’re not funny.  
Don’t do it.  
Don’t laugh at it.  
Don’t tolerate it.  
Even if it’s “just a joke.” It’s not okay. Stop it.
 
## Second Grade: The Quiet Time Experiment

My second-grade teacher attempted quiet time. It worked—for one reason only:  
If we were quiet long enough, she would fall asleep.  
If we stayed quiet, she stayed asleep.  
Even seven-year-old boys can be very quiet when mischief is the reward.  
There was always one sweet girl who would wake the teacher to end the dream time. To everyone else’s dismay.
 
### Lesson Learned:

“Never trust sweet girls”… probably isn’t the lesson.  
I may just still be bitter because I had a crush on her and never said anything.  
Maybe there’s a deeper lesson here. Or maybe, as Sherlock Holmes allegedly said, “Sometimes a cigarette butt is just a cigarette butt.”  
Maybe some stories are just funny stories.  
Or maybe I’ll understand it better by Chapter 4.5.
 
## Third Grade: The Busy Work Discovery

In third grade I discovered something about myself that I didn’t fully understand for years:  
I don’t like busy work.  
I realized that sometimes Mrs. Peterburg assigned work not to teach—but to keep us busy and quiet.  
No one likes having their time wasted. Not even eight-year-olds.
 
### **Lesson Learned:**

**Don’t waste people’s time.**  
People will do extraordinary things if they believe their effort matters.  
That’s not revolutionary. But we often forget it when leading or loving others.  
To be clear—people love wasting their own time. They will spend hours watching someone else play a video game. But they want to choose that.  
Don’t waste it for them.  
This discovery stuck with me. Jaded me, even.  
By seventh grade, I was convinced all schoolwork was busy work. I saw myself as a victim of “the system.” (I later learned most teenagers feel this way. Like everyone else, I thought I was unique.)
 
## Keyboarding and the Illusion of Intelligence

In seventh grade I took a keyboarding class.  
There, I discovered—and mastered—the art of copy and paste, thanks to WordPerfect.  
I finished assignments in a third of the time. While my classmates typed away, I played _Scorched Earth_ from a floppy disk.  
I became an expert at launching tiny pixel missiles across the screen at precise trajectories.  
What I did not master… was typing.  
Now, as I type these words while looking down at my fingers and pecking at keys, I wish I had understood the purpose of the repetition.  
I could have met the intent.
 
## Start With Why

Simon Sinek popularized “Start With Why” through his now-famous TED Talk and Golden Circle model.  
Most of us start with _what_, then move to _how_, and rarely articulate _why_.  
Critics say his New York Times bestselling book could have been a pamphlet. Maybe.  
But its power lies in its simplicity.  
Understanding _why_ does more than motivate. It shows team members how their work fits into strategic objectives. It empowers decision-making in the absence of micromanagement.  
Expressing _why_ unlocks innovation and creativity.  
I wasn’t lazy. I worked hard when I understood the purpose.
 
## The Farm, The Bikes, and Mary Poppins

I worked hard on the farm. I rode my bike a mile and a half to milk cows before I was even ten years old.  
We made games out of the ride.  
We named our bikes. Mine was called “Predator.” Mostly because it was printed on the handlebars.  
I called play-by-play like a horse race announcer:  
“And Karen on Rainbow-Rider is lagging behind—  
but here she comes with her second wind!  
She’s passing Schwinn!  
She’s coming around Husky!  
It’s neck and neck with Predator!  
Photo finish! Rainbow-Rider wins by a tire width!”  
Games, songs, stories, and water fights made the work bearable.  
Yes, it’s cheesy. Yes, it’s corny.  
But as Mary Poppins sang:  
“A spoonful of sugar helps the medicine go down.”
 
## The Pillow War Incident

Around age ten, our family stayed in a cabin for Thanksgiving. Stormy weather trapped eight rowdy kids indoors.  
No toys. No video games.  
A pillow war was inevitable.  
It escalated.  
One throw sent a pillow into the overhead light fixture. Smoke began to billow. Panic erupted. Adults scrambled for ladders and long objects.  
In the chaos, I calmly walked to the light switch and turned it off.  
Silence.  
Smoke cleared.  
Then laughter.  
“I should have thought of that.”
 
### **Lesson Learned:**

Sometimes the solution is simple.  
Sometimes it comes from an unexpected person.  
Listen to everyone.  
And don’t be afraid to suggest the simple solution yourself.
 
## Final Thoughts

This chapter carries several threads:

- Work hard and enjoy it
- Be kind
- Don’t waste time
- Explain the why
- Make hard things lighter
- Listen to simple solutions

It’s playful, but it’s building something.  
And it works.
