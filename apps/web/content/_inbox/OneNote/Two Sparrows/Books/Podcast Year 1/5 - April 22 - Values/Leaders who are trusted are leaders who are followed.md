My dad often says "It's just what we do"
 
Whether that's offering service to a neighbor, frankly forgiving and offering more grace than could possibly be expected, or driving all the way to Winnemaka because he said he would.
 
As science of it's kind often does, research into trust has revealed pretty much what we learn instinctively as kids. Basically it boils down to this.
 
Trust is consistency.
 
I know right? Duh
 
But for many of us it's a lot harder to do than it is to say. I for example and well meaning, loving, and would drop everything I'm doing at any moment to help a friend. Unless that help needs to be scheduled in advance and involves planning, then I'm about as useful as a nail gun in a Jello hanging competition. This is possibly the worst thing about me, although I'll leave it up to any future any philosophers bored enough to parse such things.
 
Every time we don't follow through, or do what we say we will it creates a bit of friction in the mind of everyone around us. That friction builds until the cost of our help in the need to hedge and plan alternatives becomes more costly than our help pays off in the first place.
 
Stephen M. R. Covey in _The Speed of Trust_ says trust functions like a multiplier. When it’s present, everything moves faster and costs less. When it’s absent, everything gets slow and expensive.
 
Trust removes a tax on the nervous system, people stop burning energy asking, _ok but is that really going to happen?_
 
There are a lot of really good reasons to not follow through on things, I feel you! Certain neurodivergent situations make this one so hard to get right.
 
Figure it out.
 
Find the ways to be the dependable and trustworthy person those around you need. And be honest and open in the process as you're getting it figured out. Don't ghost anyone, open up, and you can build that trust back over time.
 
It's just what we do.
