==This is one of the least glamorous truths in leadership, which is probably why we keep needing to relearn it. grea usually not built in the big speech, the heroic save, or the dramatic late-game moment. It is usually built in the repeated evidence of ordinary reliability.==  
==In a 1995 Academy of Management Review article that became a cornerstone of trust research, Roger Mayer, James Davis, and David Schoorman argued that people tend to judge trustworthiness through three things: ability, benevolence, and integrity. Can you do the job, do you mean well, and do your actions keep pace with your promises. You can be passionate and well liked without being trusted. You can be competent and hardworking without being trusted. Trust needs all three: competence, care, and consistency.==  
==I have come to similar conclusions watching my own successes and failures. Most of the goodwill I have built with other people, whether leading or simply trying to be a useful member of a team, has come from some mixture of competence, well-meaningness, and enough follow-through to make relying on me feel wise instead of exhausting. That last part matters more than we usually admit.==
 
==Toyota is one of those case studies business writers love so much it can make a person want to fake their own death just to avoid hearing the phrase “continuous improvement” one more time. Annoyingly, Toyota really is that useful. One of my favorite examples is the andon system. On Toyota lines, workers are expected to signal problems as they happen so abnormalities can be addressed before they grow into bigger failures. That kind of system only works when people trust that surfacing trouble early will be treated as responsible rather than disloyal. Reliability, in other words, is not just virtue. It is infrastructure.==  
==And that is worth sitting with for a second. Picture the worst low-trust, kill-the-messenger job you ever had. The one where everyone knew the problem, nobody wanted to say the problem, and leadership somehow managed to act surprised every time the problem behaved exactly like a problem. Now imagine that same workplace if people had been welcomed to raise issues early, honestly, and without fear. Feel the relief? That relief is not softness. That is what trust feels like when it becomes operational.==  
==For anyone like me who oozes well-meaningness, loves to learn, works hard, and still manages to disappoint people by aiming all that effort three inches to the left of where it actually needed to go, Scott’s Mount Fuji line is especially useful. Not all repetition is progress. Not all effort is equal. Walking to the fridge for leftover lasagna is technically taking steps, but it is not the same hike.==  
==That is part of what makes Phillippa Lally’s 2010 study on habit formation so useful here. Following people as they tried to build new daily behaviors, she found that the median time to reach near-maximum automaticity was 66 days, with substantial variation depending on the person and the behavior. Which is both less catchy than the old “21 days” line and much more annoying, mostly because it appears to be true. The habits that make people trust us usually take longer to build than we want. They are formed quietly, by repetition, and they are aimed or wasted the same way. Motion is not the same thing as direction. Walking to the refrigerator is movement. Climbing Fuji is movement. The destination matters.==
