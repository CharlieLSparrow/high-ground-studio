**[Homer’s Preface]** This book is not the first nor last book on leadership. It’s probably not the best book on leadership. It's selfish but I wrote it for myself. I wrote it to document my life. It is my story. The book began more like a journal. I suppose I started writing a memoir before I knew what a memoir was, and long before they were popular. It turned into a book on leadership when I realized that much of life is about learning, and much of what I have learned in my life is how to teach and how to lead. At times it will read more like a life-sketch. Others times it may feel similar to a self-help book about leadership. At times things may seem out of order or out of context, but life is that way. Some lessons are learned quickly, like, **don’t eat glue** - learned in Chapter One. Other lessons take a lifetime and are not realized until the pieces of life are put together and the picture in the puzzle becomes clear. Most lessons repeat themselves; sometimes as reminders and sometimes we finally understand for the first time. As you will read, many of the things I’ve learned, were learned “the hard way”, from making mistakes, learning from those mistakes, and bouncing back. I write honestly about my failings and hope that you can learn, and not make the same mistakes. One of those shortcomings was not publishing this sooner. I’ve matured immensely since I started drafts. In many instances I cringe at my own naivety, but in the end, the principals are enduring. You will make mistakes, and I will continue to error and fall short, but learning from your history and the history of others, striving for something better, and leaving a legacy, are the rewards of this life. I encourage you to write your own story, even if only for yourself. Write it down. You may discover that you’ve learned more along life’s road than you realized. And, someone else may benefit, if only to look and say, “Well, at least I don’t have his challenges” or “at least I didn’t do _that_”. I make no apologies for the writing style, only explanations. Besides, it’s my story and I’ll tell it however I want. _If you will not be forgotten after you are dead and rotten either write things worth the reading or do things worth the writing of_ –Benjamin Franklin

**Week 02 - Jan 28: The Cathedral of the Mundane**  
_Theme: Finding lessons in banana peels & bird fights_  
**[Introduction]** On Sundays at my house, growing up in Southeast Idaho, my mom, a small, thin woman with light skin but dark eyes and short dark hair, would get out of bed at 5:00 a.m. (sleeping in, compared to the rest of the week for her). At that hour she prepared roast and potatoes for Sunday dinner - not a big surprise when you have dairy cows and potato fields, but a luxury we were not allowed to take for granted. After three hours of church, and before the cows had to be milked again, I would sit around the dark wood table on big wooden chairs with my two sisters, my brother, and mom. My dad at the head of the table would ask each of us, in turn, what we learned at church that day. It was at those times that he often attempted to impart words of fatherly wisdom to his progeny.  
One day as we sat around that table in the house on bend in the road, with the rust colored linoleum beneath our feet, he told of a small swallow he saw earlier that week ferociously defending her nest from a red-tailed hawk. I stared out the big dining room window across the field with hay laying in neat rows. I lost track of the story as I watched birds of all sizes discovering rodents, bugs, and other feasts newly exposed by the freshly cut crop. His story complete, he paused. The quiet pulled me from my daydream. I had missed the point of the tale. I suppose the moral of that particular story was less important than what he shared next. He looked passed me sitting at the foot of the table across from him, passed the golden Formica countertops behind me, passed the classic gold refrigerator of that late 70s home, through the door, through the carport, and out into the pastures beyond. Then he encouraged us all to look for life lessons in everyday things. It seemed pretty simple for such dramatic effect. Intrigued, and always looking to please or impress my father.  
I watched for months but saw nothing. I tried to come up with things at random… This banana peel is like when you… get… old… or… brown spots… that… or… even throw it away... I got nothing. I figured I just didn’t see things the way my dad did. Discouraged, I almost gave up entirely. Then one night I went to see a symphony and concert at the Assembly Hall in Salt Lake City. Despite growing up on a small rural farm, my parents felt it important for us to be exposed to culture and the arts. Most of the events we attended were free, but culture building none-the-less. As I sat in that old historic building the stain glass windows impressed me. In the light of the sunset, with the music as a backdrop, the windows came alive for me. My attention turned to the program for enough time that when I recalled the windows again and looked back, the sun had set and the window were dark and dull. I thought how love and kindness shining through us makes us bright, happy, and attractive like the sun shining through those windows, but if that light goes dim, and we become selfish and self-centered, our countenance is dulled. In honesty, I do not now recall the exact analogy I made as a teenager looking out a darkened stained glass window, but I remember the elation I felt as the comparison came alive for me. I had done it. I found a life lesson in a mundane, ordinary, everyday thing.  
I continue to draw life lessons from everyday events. That is the majority of what you will read in this book; life events, underscored by lessons learned, sometimes validated by other authors and professionals. Like Aesop and his famous fables, I feel compelled to point out the moral I extrapolate from these stories and experiences. There are, of course, innumerable potential parallels and lessons to be learned. Please do not constrain yourself by my limited experience and imagination. The point and intent is that these events might inspire, encourage, motivate, or resonate in some way, whatever that is for you. I also encourage readers to **look for the encouraging, enlightening lessons in the challenging and mundane of your own lives**. Like your own modern parables, it makes for more interesting speeches and books. And it makes for a more rich and meaningful life.
 
**Week 03 - Feb 04: The Wombat & The Welder**  
_Theme: Ancestry, prison shanks, and resilience_  
**[Chapter Zero: In The Beginning]** My great grandfather’s father immigrated to the United States from Australia in the late 1800s. His father migrated to Australia from England in an era when most people from England associated Australia with criminals. I continue to search for the reasons he made that voyage. What I do know is that his descendants, my grandfather and his father, were tough men. During the great depression of the 1930s, when despair and tragedy crippled the nation, my great-grandfather, Charles Sparrow, was not selling, he was buying. He acquired a significant stretch of ground in an arid valley in South Eastern Idaho; which he cleared of sagebrush and rocks with the help of good horses and four sons.  
In the 1940s, when his other sons received agricultural deferments to stay out of the war, one boy, my grandfather, Raymond Sparrow, did not defer and was drafted into the Army. Privet Sparrow was in France guarding prisoners of war when, in an escape attempt, one of the prisoners shanked him in the abdomen with a jagged piece of metal. While recovering aboard a hospital ship, Army leaders discovered that this Idaho farm kid could weld. He then spent the rest of the war welding ships. He returned home with little fanfare; to a family farm where brothers had already staked claims. Rather than cause contention, he sought employment elsewhere. There were plenty of infrastructure projects in those days and his skills as a welder would serve him well. My aunt, in later years, presented me with parts of his Army uniform including his “Eisenhower“ jacket; which I consider today to be one of my prizest possessions. I never asked Grandpa Sparrow about his time in WWII. I never asked him much about anything, despite working side by side with him almost daily on the farm growing up. What I relayed here I know only from the little my dad shared with me after my grandfather passed away.  
I pause from story-telling at this point to impart some of the little wisdom I have, as I will do throughout the book. The second “to do” (the first being to look for the extraordinary in the ordinary and tell your own story, suggested in the introduction) is to **find out who you are, and where you came from.** I know it’s not earth shattering advice and most readers will determine by now whether to read on or add this to the stack of books they started to read but never "got into". Also, probably already obvious, I have a lot of learning to do myself; as I don’t know why my third-great-grandfather went to Australia in the first place. But truth is true, regardless of who it comes from and how simple it might be. The power of self-reflection does not cease to amaze me. Knowing who you are helps establish a starting point. It provides a lens through which to look out into the future and determine where it is you want to be. Many self-help and leadership books start here; with creating a vision. I agree that knowing where you are going is invaluable. I add, it starts with understanding where you are. And where you are, starts with knowing where you came from. Your personal back story, even your set-backs, are all part of the set-up for who you can become. (Micheal Jr.) The first lesson, spoiler alert, is tied inseparably to the last. If you understand the power of the past you also understand the power you can have on the future; the power of leaving a legacy.  
I illustrate by sharing some of the legacy of my grandpa Raymond’s bride, my Grandma, MarDene Sparrow. She sang with an amazing soprano voice. I loved to hear her sing in the church choir. She knew the words to all the hymns and didn't have to be seated on the choir benches to be heard over the congregation. She was a woman of conviction; her devotion evident as she sang praises. Grandma Sparrow left a legacy of combining these two ideas of; finding motivational stories in everyday life and remembering the stories of the past.

When the folks were first married they bought a piece of land at the mouth of Mink Creek. It was 60 acres with a good water right. The legend of Chub and Jack runs deeper than the solitary conquering of a snow bank. While assisting with the construction of the twin lakes canal, the entrance to a tunnel collapsed, trapping Jack inside. Grandpa Hansen rushed to breach a hole through which he passed food and water for weeks while men and horses cleared dirt and rocks to reopen the tunnel and free the trapped animal. Jack was eventually rescued from the tomb where he was nearly buried alive but not without lasting effects. The weeks in near pitch black darkness left him ultra sensitive to light and nearly blind. He relied heavily on the commands of his handler and the feeling of the teammate next to him to know where to walk and when to pull. Chub remained a loyal companion, guiding him around the yard to avoid obstacles, to food, and to water. And Jack didn’t quit. Despite his disability he remained useful, helpful, and strong. My connection to this tale is deeply personal, as I face my own challenges and rely heavily on my teammates to, some times, literally guide me through. I will spare the details at this point because some are yet to raw to share, but challenge you to derive your own interpretation and meaning from the story, if you haven’t already. The lessons from these two are endless and legendary.  
**[Charlie's Sidebar: The Punchline]** **Michael Write Jr, or simply "Michael Jr", is a comedian who, in his routine and in his book "Funny How Life Works", describes what he considers to be the process of comedy. For him, that punchline is to deliver an opportunity to laugh. He further proposes that our best punchline will be when use our setup in a surprisingly helpful way. I agree. **Week 04 - Feb 11: The School of Hard Knocks**  
_Theme: Innovation: The "Predator" bike & pillow fire_  
**[Chapter One: The Early Days]** My earliest memories are of school. The earliest is of preschool, running around in circles. I remember the song we sang about it, “See me run, running around in circles, running and running, running around the room”. Songs are a powerful way to remember things. Vivid images, smells, and emotions also connect those synapses in the brain in binding ways that make them forever easy to recall. I remember in pre-school a kid named Cody eating my cousin Andy’s glue; that evokes some vivid images, smells, and emotions. I learned a lot from Mrs. Kathy at Kerr Preschool. I learned and worked hard, but I didn’t know that I was learning or working hard. I simply enjoyed it. Lesson learned: **work hard and enjoy it.** That is a theme comes up again and again in my life. I have not ever been the smartest, strongest, most attractive, or most social person in the ”office“, but being a person that others can count on, because they know you will contribute meaningful effort, makes me a valuable member of the team. For the other part, having fun does not have to mean implementing recess and playing on the merry-go-round at lunch (although it doesn’t need to be ruled out either). It does mean that the work environment should be enjoyable enough for workers not to prefer to get in a car accident accompanied by a trip to the hospital on the way to work in order to avoid setting foot in the building. (Please tell me I’m not the only one to have felt that way before.) It doesn’t require giggling pillow fights or slides between floors to be fun, although I love that idea (The giggling fights not the silly slide idea, of course). It does require some recognition of humanity, which is possible in all organizations, yes, including serious and dangerous organizations such as the military. I don’t remember much about the first grade, only that my teacher Mrs. Terman quickly became known as Mrs. “Termite”; which is hilarious when you’re a six year old boy. Being the recipient of such ridicule is, however, less humorous. Called names, or told, “Scotty, Watty, needs to go Potty”, sounds ridiculous now, but devastated my six year old self. Lesson learned: **Be kind**. No one likes to be called names or belittled. It seems like a simple lesson but apparently, many leaders did not learn in the first grade that bullying and hazing along with, sexist, racial, comments, ethnic slurs and innuendos are not appropriate. Don’t do it. Don’t laugh at it. Don’t tolerate it. Even if it is sarcastic or in jest, it is no ok, it’s not funny, stop it! My second grade teacher attempted quiet time; which worked only because if we were quiet enough, for long enough, she would fall asleep, and if we stayed quiet, she would stay asleep. Even 7 year old boys can be quite if it means getting away with mischief. There was always one sweet girl who would burst the bubble of dream time by waking the teacher, to everyone else’s dismay. Lesson learned: never trust sweet girls… That’s probably not it. I’m probably just upset because I totally had a crush on her and I never said anything. Maybe there is a better lesson here for me or someone else to glean; then again, it may be true that “sometimes a cigaret butt is just a cigaret butt"(attributed to Sherlock Holmes) in other words, maybe sometimes a funny story is just that, an entertaining, funny story. Or, maybe I will understand this story better by the time I get to chapter 4.5. I discovered something about myself in the third grade that I didn’t understand well until many years later and I was not able to have a handle on it until years after that. I learned that I didn’t like busy work. I discovered, at the age of 8, that sometimes my teacher, Mrs. Peterburg, gave us things to do for the sole purpose of keeping us busy and, by default, quiet. No one likes to have their time wasted; not even an 8 year olds. Lesson learned: **Don’t waste people’s time**, it is precious to them. Regular people will do extraordinary things if they believe it is useful. That’s not earth shattering news but we don’t always apply it to those we love or lead by that golden rule. Make no mistake, people love to waste their own time. They are experts at it. They will spend every available hour watching videos of other people playing a game or lining up colored shapes on a tiny screen. But they want to do it themselves, don’t waste it for them. This discovery of busy work stuck with me, jaded me even. By the time I reached the seventh grade I was convinced that all school work was busy work. I convinced myself that I was a victim of “the system” (I would find out many years later that most teenagers feel that way; somehow, like everyone else, I thought I was the only one.) In that year I took a keyboarding class. In that class I discovered, and subsequently became a master of, the “copy and paste”, made possible by the advent of WordPerfect. I finished my work in a third of the time as my classmates, whom I thought were not nearly as smart as me. While they typed away, I played “Scorched Earth” off of a floppy disk I brought to class with me. I mastered sending a tiny blip across the screen at the right trajectory to hit the opposing player’s tiny pixels in the shape of a tank. I did not, however, master typing. By the time I realized the purpose for the repetitive typing of the same letters and sentences over and over, it was much later, and by later, I mean, too late. Even now as I type these words, looking at my fingers and pecking for the keys, I wish that I had understood the purpose of the task. I could have met the intent. Simon Sinek is synonymous with the leadership and life principle of “**Start with Why**”. With his, now famous, “golden circle” he described in a TED Talk how most of us start with ”what” and then ”how”, but rarely articulate the reason why. The video went viral, and continues to garner millions of views, confirming the need for someone to describe this simple, yet vital, principle. But understanding why goes far beyond motivation. Critics of Sinek’s New York Times best selling book, complain that it could have been a pamphlet. What makes the concept powerful is how simple it is. I wish to emphasize how understanding why helps team members see how what they do fits in to the overall strategic objectives. This is paramount to knowing what to do In the absence of specific instruction about every possible scenario. It is what allows a leader to lead and to manage without micromanaging. Expressing why is the key because it allows for innovation, creativity, and individual skills to achieve objectives. I was not a lazy kid. I did great work when I enjoyed it and when I bought into the reason for it. I worked hard on the farm, often times riding my bike to the barn a mile and a half away to milk the cows without supervision before my age had double digits. I did make up games to make long bike rides bearable. We had names for our bikes. Mine received the ingenious name “Predator”. Probably because it was written on the padding of the handlebars. I would call out the play by play of the ride as if I were an announcer at a horse race. “And Karen riding on Rainbow-Rider is lagging behind but here she comes with her second wind! She’s passing Schwinn! She’s coming around Husky! And it’s neck ’a neck with Predator! Here they come across the finish line! It will be a photo finish folks! Yes, it’s Rainbow-Rider over Predator by a tire width! Unbelievable! No one expected it! She’s making a victory lap waving to the crowd. Games, singing songs, telling stories, or a good water fight, always made the hours go better. **Yes cheesy, yes corny but true just the same as Mary Poppins sang, A spoon full of sugar helps the medicine go down.** – Mary Poppins When I was about ten years old my family stayed in a cabin with my Mom’s sister and her family for Thanksgiving. Stormy weather kept eight rowdy kids inside. No video games or toys in the cabin made a pillow war inevitable. Though not a regular “fight” it definitely escalated to a conflict of epic proportions.. One fateful throw landed a pillow from a loft into the light fixture high above the floor. Smoke soon billowed from the super-heated pillow case. Panic followed, as the object was too high to reach. Children screamed and adults scrambled for things to stand on and objects long enough to reach the pillow before it burst into flames. In the chaos I calmly walked to the light switch and turned it off. Everyone froze, and the room went silent for a moment. The smoke began to clear. Then everyone broke into laughter with expressions of “I should have thought of that”. The truth is that, sometimes the solution to an issue within your organization is simple. Sometimes it comes from an unexpected person. **Don’t be afraid to listen to everyone and don’t be afraid to suggest the simple solution you have either.**
 
**Week 05 - Feb 18: The Values Toolkit**  
_Theme: Character - The dog, spilled milk, frozen flashlight_  
**[Chapter Two: Values: Get Some]** As my siblings played in the barnyard on a summer day, the dry summer air seemed to blow in an unknown friendly dog from out of nowhere. She was a hyper pup with long hair, a long nose, and a perpetually happy face. She was no Sheppard, Collie, or Sheepdog of any kind. Clearly not a working dog at all, defiantly nothing like the red healer, Bandit, we had that could bring the cows in from the corral for milking with one “get ‘em”. She was clearly a stray, and clearly a mutt. But when my little brother played the puppy dog eyes on my dad, Dad agreed that we could run an ad in the paper and if no one claimed the thing, Charlie could keep it. When after weeks of asking around and running ads proved fruitless, the promise to keep the pup was honored and Dad gave in. A black end to her all white tail gave her the name Tip, or Tippie, as Aunt Dena Rae called her, and she became part of the family. She wasn’t much for herding cows, in fact Grandpa would often lock her in the granary when livestock needed to be herded, because she would run around and scatter the animals rather than direct them. Even with all the effort to show her what to do, she just wanted to play. But she couldn’t even play fetch, dumb mutt. It didn’t matter; my little brother loved her. Then, one day, Tip was nowhere to be fund. She was not at the barn, not at grandma's house, not in the fields or the pasture, or at the neighbor down the dusty road. She had disappeared as suddenly as she had appeared. Kids cried and prayed. One night about a week after Tip was lost my little brother made an odd request. “Dad,” he pleaded with the same puppy eyes he used to keep the dog in the first place, “will you go get Tippie no matter how far away she is.” “Of course,” the answer came back quickly. For surly, how far away could the little dog be. The very next day the phone rang and my dad answered, "Hello". “Is this Charlie Sparrow?" asked the woman at the other end. "He is at school… This is his father…. May I help you?" relied my dad, unsure why an unfamiliar lady would call for a first grader. "Sir, I am calling from the pound in Winnemucca, Nevada”. (It's a real place. Look it up, I had to.) ”We found a dog with a tag indicating it belongs to a Charlie Sparrow… we are sorry we didn’t call sooner but the fir around the dog’s neck made it so that we hadn’t seen the tags until today.” Now, Winnemucca, NV is a seven hour drive from Weston, ID, one way. As near as my dad could figure, Tip had jumped into the back of Grandpa’s old brown farm truck, as he always did when it pulled out of the yard. Accept, this time it was not Grandpa driving down the lane to feed the calves. Uncle Bob had a tendency to “borrow” things, and this time he borrowed the truck. He must have stopped for gas and the thirsty pup, after a 7 hour ride, must have jumped out for a much needed pit stop. It's hard to say the exact sequence of events, but at this point, what was a guy to do? For one, he could have ignored that call. After all, he was the only one who knew where the dog was. He could have easily told the nice lady on the phone from Winnemucca, ”Ma’am, you just go ahead and take off those tags that say he belongs to Charlie Sparrow and give that hyper, long haired, mutt to some other nice kid”. He could have found a much better dog as a replacement. He could have, but instead, he didn't hesitate, he got in his truck and drove for 14 hours, the rest of the day and late into night, to Winnemucca, Nevada to get it. Mom didn't tell us why dad had not come he for dinner or why we needed to do the milking. It wasn’t uncommon for us to take care of the chores so we did’t ask. I was awakened at some point in the early morning hours as Tip rushed through the house licking faces in beds and checking to see that all kids were accounted for. That dog was queen of the house after that. Even Mom, who was famous in our house for raising her voice only one time that any of us could ever remember, to chastise, "Tip! That will be enough!", even Mom let the dog in the house on cold winter nights in Idaho. She even made him a spot on the carpet of all things. But the real hero was Dad. My father was a man who kept promises. I never liked that dog. My dad certainly didn’t drive to Nevada for me, and I never asked him to. But that day I knew he would. I knew he could be trusted “no matter how far” he had to go. **Leaders who are trusted are leaders who are followed.** _Character is doing the right thing when nobody's looking. There are too many people who think that the only thing that's right is to get by, and the only thing that's wrong is to get caught. ~J.C. Watts_  
We had a small plot of 20 acres behind the house and my grandpa had 40 acres behind his place. With the 30 acres we leased from the neighbors, it kept a boy with some work to do in the summer. My dad worked for the West Cache Canal Company bringing water across the high Southern Idaho step desert to the thirsty sandy fields. It was a bit unpredictable where he might be on a given day but he was always predictably busy through the summer months. In addition, the milk from the dairy was our family’s livelihood. Dad used to say things like, “cow’s don’t care if you’re busy or tired.” “They don’t care if it’s Saturday or a holiday or your birthday.” ”They have to be milked, twice a day, every day.” “There are no days off”. On his busy days my dad would load our bikes in the back of the old white pick up truck and drop my sister and I off at the barn. With the cows milked we would ride the mile and a half back home. At the end of the day, if it didn’t look like dad would make it home for milking, we would ride the bikes back down to the barn to milk the cows again. My dad trusted my sister and me with incredible responsibility for pre-teen adolescence. For the most part we made him proud, but we did have our moments. When we cleaned out the lines after milking we removed the line from the tank to flush a rinse cycle, which we fed to the calves, and then ran the cleaning solution through. At the beginning of each milking the line would be reconnected to the tank. I remember one evening when the milking was entirely completed, I went into the wash room to remove the line from the tank. The line was already removed and I knew immediately by the milk splashed around the drain that it had not been recently removed by my sister. We had literally dumped the entire milking down the drain. My dad was rightfully upset when we called him over the CB radio to let him know what had happened. But I was surprised at how quickly we were forgiven and confidence was restored. It was clearly accidental. He truly trusted us and he needed us. Leaders have a responsibility to remove form their organization individuals who have a habit of doing things that are not beneficial and/or detrimental to the organization. This must be done for two reasons. The first is obvious, replace the person who is not pulling their weight in order to put a more productive person in their stead. The second is maybe less obvious, but actually just as, if not more, important. You have to cut out cancerous, bad strawberries from destroying the moral of the rest of the workers. It can be extremely de-motivating to see someone doing a poor or even mediocre job who receives the same treatment, pay, rewards, etc. as someone who is working much harder. Keeping under performers around brings everyone to that low level of expectation. It is extremely difficult sometimes as a leader not to reward poor performance with indifference and to reward hard work with more work. Standards must be clearly written and disseminated to all. Failure to meet standards must be dealt with in a swift and severe way. However, individuals who make mistakes and work hard to correct and reduce mistakes should be forgiven. This creates an atmosphere of honesty. When mistakes are made, which will happen, it is important to have a climate where it can be confessed wholly and honestly. We as leaders must be willing to admit our own short comings and mistakes as well. Forgiving subordinates fosters forgiveness from them when we make mistakes, which we will do. **Forgive and Fire, both are critical.** In _Chapter 4: When to Mentor, When to Fire_ of a relevant book on leadership titled “The Dichotomy of Leadership”, the former Navy Seal authors, Jocko Willink and Leif Babin, address this challenging dichotomy. They share stories of Seal teammates whom they rallied around to great success. They also share the story of a teammate who they needed and cared about but who ultimately did not meet a standard level of proficiency and was removed from the teams. Things to consider: The initial default should focus on coaching, teaching, mentoring, and counseling. Turn over is costly in so many ways, not the least of which is the time and emotional efforts of reforming teams. Efforts to retain should emphasize an individuals strengths and how they can be utilized effectively. Be conscious not to remove someone from your team simply because you don’t “get along”. Diversity is a valuable asset. Check yourself; are you dismissing this person because they disagree with you? Surrounding yourself with all “Yes” men and women, can be hurting the potential of the organization. On the other hand, I have unfortunately waited to long, on occasion, to let someone go, thinking I was helping the individual, thinking I was helping the team. I later recognized the responsibility of firing was mine alone, as the leader, and I had hurt the team by not taking charge of it. The good news is that if you have done the formal, written counseling and can demonstrate what expectations were and where the individual failed to meet those expectations, if you have clearly communicated the consequences of not meeting objectives, then the firing feels more logical and less threatening to both the one doing the firing and the one leaving. Removing someone from your team does not have to be painful or dramatic. Unless they have done something illegal or un ethical it can simply be an opportunity for them to pursue options that are a better fit for their skills.  
Work on the farm was an everyday affair. As expressed previously, cows had to get milked every day, twice a day, no exceptions. It would be many years later that I would understand that greatness is typically forged, not in one great act, but from meaningful, often monotonous, things done every day. Even great moments and achievements are generally reached by doing mundane things for a long time before the recognition or reward. People don’t just wake up doctors; years of school, days and nights of residency, hours upon hours of study and papers later, the results are ultimately manifest. To be clear, it is critical that those mundane, everyday acts are steps on the path toward the objective. Mount Fuji can be climbed by putting one foot in front of the other, but one foot in front of the other across the kitchen floor to the refrigerator does not get one to the top of Fuji. The key is to **build habits**, to be consistent, but know when to deviate. No I didn’t learn this lesson at 14. Nor can I say with confidence that I get it correct now. But those good habits can be started young and build upon over a lifetime. My dad sold the farm when I was 15, just before I was old enough that he would let me to drive the tractor in the corral. It seemed everything ended just before I was old enough to do it. I had looked forward to riding a motorcycle. Just before I turned 12 and my dad would allow me to ride it, my grandpa sold the bike and bought a four-wheeler. Ok to drive, but not the same. I harbored ill feeling towards my father for a long time for all the things I thought he should have provided me, and for all the wisdom I thought he should have imparted to me. He knew more about cars, and engines, and wielding, and life in general that he didn't teach me. I understand now that he did his best and I love and appreciate him for that. **Holding grudges and blaming others for doing something, or not doing something, is not helpful. Grudges lead only to mistrust and contention.** Ironically the reason for the tension and animosity is rarely even know by those for whom the grudge is held. I held a stupid grudge for years that my sister got to attend my birthday party and I didn’t get to attend hers. When she reads this, it will probably be the first time she would even guess that this happened. I probably wouldn’t have enjoyed her birthday party anyway. But it was the principle of the matter. It seems silly now but the emotions were real and raw and long lasting. Unfortunately, **communication** is not some silver bullet or magic solution to all problems, nor the answer to all leadership challenges, but there is a reason it is included in nearly all leadership courses. It is an important and powerful tool. If you haven’t noticed by now, there are people on this planet, and to be a leader, other people will be involved. Direct communication about desires and concerns will go a long way to mitigate unmet expectations and misunderstandings. My father and I now have a great relationship. I believe this is not attributable to any great event but a significant investment in time and a willingness to listen. I remember one cold winter night looking down into a dark hole, which I believe was the water box at the spring that supplied water to our house. The water running right out of the frozen ground was only not frozen itself because it was in motion. The pump had stopped, probably because of the cold. I stood over the hole looking down at my dad who hung onto a cold aluminum ladder with the inside of his elbow in order to free his hands to work. My job should have been simple, hold the flashlight. But despite the simplicity of the task I was not doing a great job. I was having trouble because I was trying to tell some tall tale that required demonstrating what was happening in the story as much as telling it. My flailing arms were not holding consistent light on the frozen pump. My dad eventually took the light from me to hold under his chin while he continued to work with both hands. I was grateful because my hand was getting cold from holding the light and I was having a hard time making the exaggerated hand motions necessary for the dramatic nature of my story with the flashlight in my hand. At some point -20* F temperature was too much to even stand and flail my arms. I climbed in the truck and cranked up the heat, leaving him alone in the dark frozen hole struggling to get water to his family. Sorry dad. Thanks for listening. Sometimes the best investment you can make is to **listen**. Working summer months for the West Cache Cannel Company required long hours of driving down bumpy dirt roads along the cannel banks regulating the water flow and insuring everyone took their allotted turn . My dad often invited me to join him; which I was glad to do. On such occasions, it was my job to periodically jump out of the truck to open the gates of the farmers along the route. Then after my dad drove the truck through, I would quickly close the gate, jump back in the truck, and we would again be on our way. The time was passed listening to the radio and drinking lemon-aid. Most of the time passed without conversation and most of the conversations we did have were not thought provoking or earth shattering but it left the window open to have important conversations. Your time is valuable but if you don’t give any of that time to work alongside those you lead you will miss out on deeply understanding your subordinates and gaining their trust. **Invest in them your time**. With the cows gone, I felt a need for employment and income. My sister had been hired at the local fast-food place. It seemed logical to apply at the same place. I worked hard and did a good job, but was disappointed that my boss never saw in me the potential for leadership that she saw in others. It turns out that we can’t all be in charge. **Some are ready sooner than others, some are never ready to lead.** **Even hard workers don’t always make good leaders.** Between work and playing in the pep band at all of the high school sporting events from football, basketball, and even a few volleyball games, I loved to be in the school and community plays. It seemed even more disingenuous to kiss her as practice for the show. The bottom line, I was a good kid, very naïve, but a good kid. And I didn't get into trouble. I did push my autonomy too far. One night, after a show, Cindy and I hung out behind the theater in the parking lot long after the curtain closed. He looked at Cindy and softened his tone a little, but still quite sternly stated "your parents are probably worried about you too." That's all he said. That's all he had to say. It was after 2:00 am. Message received, we quickly said goodbye and went home. **Leaders, give subordinates the freedom to succeed but you still have to care and make corrections when necessary.** **Subordinates, give your leader reason to trust you.**

Grandpa handed me back my shotgun as the “atta-boys” erupted as well as the “lucky shot” comments, “you'll never do that again”, “bet you can't do that again” was said over and over. And he had! No amount of encouraging changed his mind. He said little but he did say that it appeared to him that we were the ones that needed the practice. What would have happened with the next three shots? No one will ever know. But what I do know is Grandpa Francis was a great shot and he knew when he had made his point indisputably perfect. Like Kenny Rogers says “You've got to know when to hold 'em, know when to fold 'em, **know when to walk away**, and know when to run. You never count your money when you're sittin' at the table. There'll be time enough for countin' when the dealin's done.”
   

**WEEK 09 - MAR 18: THE VOMIT COMET** _(Text: Chapter Three - Part 4)_  
The thing I struggled with the most at Basic was not the physical or mental challenges. I struggled most with people who could not follow directions. Too many times I recall sitting in an empty room after our drill sergeant gave specific instructions to “clean weapons and be quiet.” Whispering would start, which escalated to quite talking. Then shushing would follow, followed by the shushing of the shushers. Egos got in the way of reason, and arguments ignited over who should “shut up” and who couldn’t tell who to “shut up”. "Shhhhhh." "you shhhhh" ”I don't have to shhhhh.” ”You don't tell me what to do." The drill sergeant would return, make us all do push-ups, then leave with the same instructions as before, “clean weapons and be quiet”. As soon as he left, the fighting would commence again. “We wouldn’t get smoked if you would stop talking”. “You stop talking”. “Why don’t you both stop talking” a third party would chime in. “Why don’t you butt out”. "Why don't you shut up"… It was almost a relief when the drill sergeant would come in again and we would exercise until we couldn’t possibly move any part of our bodies anymore. Then he would leave and it would start all over again. Egos and selfishness sometimes overrule reason and sound judgment. **You cannot allow pride to affect your decisions.** If you do, you will inevitably make poor decisions. You also cannot have a culture of selfishness in a successful organization. Competition? Yes! But when people feel the need to save face despite negative consequences they make rash choices and tear teams apart. One person that never seemed to stop talking was Privet Simmons. With the last name “Sparrow”, I stood right behind PVT Simmons in every line. He would tell me all about his pearl-white Volkswagen Beatle with royal purple shag interior. He went on and on in such detail that I still remember 30 years later. He had participated in JROTC in high school and thought himself to be an expert of all things Army. I was constantly in trouble because Simmons was constantly turned around talking to me. Toward the end of Basic Combat Training, while conducting our field training exercise, the company sat on the outside of a bend in a dry river bed. On the opposite bank our company commander stood talking. The grassy slope curved around perfectly into a natural, miniature amphitheater. The raised bank on the other side made for a natural stage. This was the company’s new commander, not that I had seen the old commander more than once. He was giving some “Hooah” speech that I was less than interested in. - In the Army it is customary to say “Hooah” as a response. Some argue that “Hooah” is actually ”H-U-A”, and like most things in the Army, it is an acronym which stands for Heard Understood and Acknowledged. Whatever it means, it can be somewhat motivating at times but mostly becomes droning and mindless. This commander seemed to prefer "Check" as a replacement to the traditional "Hooah" and was saying “Check” after every sentence; to which we responded with “Check, Sir”. As I sat thinking more about saying “Check” verses “Hooah” than about what was being said, I got the sense that something wasn’t right. All was quiet, too quiet, and although I had heard my share of boring speeches in my life, this one seemed to be dragging out intentionally. He seemed to be stalling for something. I felt uneasy, and while the cool grass invited me to lounge on one elbow as the rest of the Soldiers did, I sat ridged looking around. As I observed, I noticed the drill sergeants in the back passing each other and shooting glances that said to me that something was definitely up but that they were trying to be discrete about it. I elbowed my battle buddy PVT Lennon and signaled to him that something was off, I’m not sure he understood but sat up as well. Then I heard it, a sharp pop followed by a loud hiss; the distinct sound of a CS canister. I reacted without thought. I opened my M40 protective mask case on my left hip with my left hand. I reached in with my right hand and grabbed it firmly. My chin went into the chin rest. My left hand came up to pull the straps tightly onto my head. With my right hand I cleared and sealed the mask. The Army standard to do this is nine seconds. It took me less than four. But now I was certain that I failed… and died. It was completely black and I wasn’t breathing. The first step to dawning the M40 protective mask is to close your eyes and hold your breath, which I was still doing. “Breath!” I told myself. I gasped a first breath. I was alive, but I was still confused by the darkness. “My eyes are closed,” I finally acknowledged to myself. I opened my eyes, but to my surprise it was still dark. Then I felt the distinct burn on the back of my neck where the CS gas began to react with the sweat on my skin. “My hood!” I suddenly realized, was still over the front of my mask. I pulled it over my head and looked out for the first time. I immediately looked to my left to where my battle buddy, PVT Lennon, had been sitting next to me. He looked at me in that same instant having situated his mask simultaneously with me. “Are you good” I asked, muffled through the mask, showing him a questioning thumbs-up. He returned the thumbs-up gesture and muffled through his mask “I’m good. Are you good?” “I’m good,” I said shaking my thumbs-up hand to emphasize. “Good.” He repeated shaking his thumbs-up as we stared at each other. Then slowly my focus shifted past my comrade to what was happening beyond him and all around us. It looked like children were let loose in a hen house and were trying to catch chickens. PVT Lennon and I seemed to be the only two people still sitting and not running around franticly. The scene reminded me of a clip from a cartoon. I watched as one Soldier disoriented, eyes blurred from the gas, and panicked, ran straight on into a large tree. He fell hard on his back, scrambled to his feet again, and ran off into the woods bouncing off trees like a pin ball and out of sight. I watched a female soldier throw the crutches she had hobbled around with and run into the woods in another direction. I watched two people literally run right into each other, both falling backwards and onto their back sides on the ground. “This is crazy,” I thought “It’s like a ridiculous play I’m watching.” Then, as if on queue, I saw PVT Simmons run in from stage right. He stopped center stage, right on the spot where the commander had stood speaking moments before. “Where did that guy go”, I wondered. Simmons was coughing and hacking. As he stood there he reached for and struggled with his mask. He tried to put it on but the effects of the gas had already overcome him. He looked as though he would throw up. Then the mask was on his face. But it was too late. I could see his face clearly, as he looked directly at me through the mask lenses in panic. His eyes grew wide. His body contracted. Then, vomit filled the lower half of his mask, up passed his nose. He couldn’t breathe. His body convulsed. I could see his eyes just over the slurry. They pleaded with me for help. There was nothing I could do but watch. His body trembled like a volcano rumbling before an eruption. His eyes grew wider and then, suddenly, disappeared as he vomited again filling the entire mask. He flopped and twitched and tore the mask from his face, gasping for breath; then finally breathing in deep, forcing gas into his already burning lungs. He bent over throwing up again and again. He tried to run. He stumbled a few steps, vomited, ran a few more, stopped briefly to bend over and vomit again. As he disappeared into the woods I imagined the burning of his face as the gas reacted to the wet mess that was all over him. I couldn’t dwell long. As I turned to my right I saw a drill sergeant approaching fast. I prepared myself for an encounter, seeing him coming towards me and not yet understanding why. Then it became crystal clear. In the panic and chaos Soldier’s gear was strung across the area. Helmets, masks, cases, weapons, and anything he could see, he was after it. I suddenly felt a need to protect it. I began grabbing everything I could get my hands on. PVT Lennon saw what was happening and quickly joined the race. The drill sergeant saw me, he saw what I was doing, and he began working faster. We moved towards each other both determined to get as much stuff as we could. Both with arms full, clinging to straps, scrambling. Then it looked as though we would reach the same last weapon at the same time. I reached out and grabbed it the instant before he did, but his hand was on it. I clung tightly determined to save whoever the owner was from certain destruction at the hand of the drill sergeant. He looked hard at me. Noting my determination he released it with a shove and moved on in search of treasure elsewhere. I couldn’t carry more stuff if I found it. I looked at Lennon who had also collected more than he could comfortably carry. The sun was setting and people were gathering back together. Many covered in vomit looked exhausted and glad the ordeal was finally over. Word got out that Sparrow and Lennon had collected a bunch of stuff. A line formed and many cheered and hugged us as they got their things back. One poor sole at the end of the line was devastated to learn that we did not have his weapon. Then he heard his weapon number being called out by the drill sergeant. He hung his head and slowly moved that direction, not wanting to face the consequences of leaving his weapon. I felt bad for those who faired so poorly through the ordeal. At the same time I couldn’t help but feel proud of the way my battle buddy and I had reacted. I saw Simons still wet from his own fluids. He looked drained. I felt bad for him too, but I couldn’t help but feel somewhat vindicated. I smiled, I knew I shouldn’t, but THAT was the funniest thing I had ever seen in real life. Basic training was almost at its end and Simmons didn't have many more opportunities to turn around and get us both in hot water but when he did, I had only to remind him of standing in the middle of that clearing and throwing up in his mask. “Remember that time?” He didn't want to talk about it. And that kept him quiet. **Lessons learned: 1-Learn your job to the point that it becomes second nature. Plan for the crisis so that when it comes you are prepared and even surprise yourself by how well you react. 2- Be vigilant. 3- Trust your gut about things that don’t seem right. 4- Help other people, even if you don’t have to. Helping others leads to mutual help. Call it Karma or whatever you want to call it. Helping others always returns good works to you. Karma works in the other way too. The deserving eventually get what they deserve.**
   

**WEEK 10 - MAR 25: THE SWEAR JAR** _(Text: Chapter Three - Part 5)_  
One of the first things people noticed about me at basic training was the way I talked. Bobby Little, from Boston (whose name backwards is Little, Bobby), and Privet Young from Mississippi (who had no rhythm and could not march) had more distinct accents but it was more about what I didn’t say than what I did say. I didn’t tell dirty jokes or use foul language. Many Soldiers asked me why I didn’t; arguing that they are “just words” and that “it doesn’t matter”. I didn’t argue. I agreed. They are just words, and I _just chose_ not to use them. When I began my Advanced Individual Training some of the guys that went with me to basic training joined me. I remember vividly the first Sunday morning at AIT. I heard from across the bay “SPARROW WHAT?!”, “WHERE IS HE?”, “SPARROW!” PVT Drummond, a giant black guy, came into view as I moved to the center of the room. I saw that he brought company, all the people he had established as his pose over the first few days of being there. The rest of the bay of 60 people gathered around to see what was going on. I didn’t know what he wanted but he sounded angry. “You Sparrow?” he glared at me. “Yep,” I said as cheerful as I dared. “Green tells me you don’t G*^ D@#& swear.” “Nope,” I replied, again as cheerfully as I thought I could get away with. PVT Drummond spent what seemed like an eternity swearing words that I had never heard, and describing in the most disgusting way how little he thought of me. Then he stopped, looked hard at me, and walked away. The crowd dispersed murmuring. I went back to what I was doing, confused about what had just happened and why. I sent one up that I was still breathing out of my face and not a hole in my chest. Then I didn’t think much more about it. A few weeks later I stood in a formation with my platoon. The drill sergeants gave us instructions, as they always did, to stand still and not talk. I thought they were simple instructions, but the same meat head that never understood that “don’t talk” meant “don’t open and close your mouth while vibrating your vocal cords”, started trying to be funny and telling dirty jokes. I turned and looked at him and boldly said “shut up”. (Did I mention PVT Jones weighed in around 2 “fity” at 6’ 3”, dwarfing my 5’ 10” 145lbs with my rucksack on?) “What did you say to me?” he said shocked. I immediately regretted my approach. I had probably been too harsh, but I had already committed. “You heard what I said,’ Shut Up!’” I looked at him, “I am tired of getting smoked because you can’t keep your mouth shut and stand still.” In the Army, especially during entry level training, group punishment is the norm. If one person is out of line, everyone pays. This guy had caused us all to do any number of strenuous exercises, known as “getting smoked”, on multiple occasions for not holding his tongue. The conversation ended quickly as the drill sergeants returned. When the formation ended, I went to my bunk quickly followed by PVT Jones and the rest of the platoon. “Do you want to fight me, Sparrow?” he scowled looking down at me and puffing out his chest. I looked up and calmly replied. “No, I don’t want to fight you. It wouldn’t be much of a fight. You would crush me.” I hesitated, then determined that I was already in this and continued. ”I am just sick and tired of getting smoked because you can’t shut your mouth in formation.” “You’re just weak and can’t hack it,” he retorted. “I can do the push-ups,” I told him, “I can do the exercises. I just don’t want to have to, and we wouldn’t have to, if we would all just do what we’re supposed to do, and keep quiet in formation.” I knew at any minute this guy was going to feed me my teeth. I had visions of blacking out and waking up on the floor. Then something unexpected happened. PVT Drummond stepped between us. “Come on Jones. You just can’t talk sense into this guy,” and he pushed him away. Nothing more was ever said to me about either of those incidences. But I was again grateful to have the same number of holes in my face, and Jones did tone it down a bit. There was another Soldier of my faith in 44 Truck, PVT Johnson. He tried hard to fit in and make friends. He relaxed his standards. No, he didn’t do anything unconscionable, or even pick up any addictive habits. He just decided to use foul language and laugh at the off colored jokes. What happened surprised me. I became a person who was respected and admired for having principals and living by them. PVT Johnson became a laughing stock; mocked for having no discipline or backbone to follow what he claimed to be right. I don’t know that this is worth much more than a good story for anyone else. But for me it represents more than a story. It represents who I am. I couldn’t swear now if I wanted to. I used the word “pissed” to describe how upset I was in an email I sent once and I lost a little respect from the some of the foulest mouthed people. Probably writing it here got a rise, or maybe a chuckle, from a few of you. But for some, who have never heard me say it, I may have even lost a little credibility. I have a reputation to uphold. I am respected for having discipline and control of myself, including my tongue. Where does this leave me as a leader? No, I don’t have a swearing jar, nor am I advocating for it. I don’t point my finger at anyone and call them a heathen, that's not helpful and would only make me look arrogant. They just know that I don’t talk like that. Have I heard crude things said that I thought were funny? Sure. Do I respect and admire people who also cuss? Absolutely. My grandpa had a farmer’s tongue. I’m in the military and a combat veteran. I expect to hear Soldier and Sailor words, but I do not tolerate racial or sexual comments no matter how sarcastic or humorous they are meant to be. There is no room for bigotry on a team. Eliminating offensive language and degrading comments from the work place makes it an environment people want to work in. **Demonstrating discipline earns respect and leads to disciplined followers.**
   

**WEEK 11 - APR 01: THE TREADMILL FACTORY** _(Text: Chapter Three - Part 6)_  
After Basic Training I went home for a few months and began a job at Icon Health and Fitness making treadmills on a production line. I worked on a team with one other person. She would start the treadmill for the first time, checking functionality. I would put on the back roller and then adjust the belt. It was at my station that any issues with the machine were often discovered for the first time. When something had a problem, the machine was moved off the production line and onto the “mechanics loop”. The mechanic would fix the issue and move it back onto the line or assemble the machine alone before sending it through the quality control booth. I quickly learned that I could fix many of the issues myself without sending the half assembled machine off to be someone else's problem. I also discovered that by doing so I could save a lot of time in the production process. I found, for example, that if a bad batch of circuit boards were put into the treadmills that the workers putting in circuit boards simply continued to put them on and it took a long time before the problem was finally cut off at the source. If I identified the issue, I could stop bad circuit boards from being put in, quickly change out those already on the line and keep production running. My line leaders saw in me an ability to work independently and when a position came available to bring parts to the line I was offered the parts puller job. I enjoyed very much being off the grind of the line. I also made a few more cents an hour, a big perk for me as I was working to save money. With me off the production line, the mechanic became inundated with machines. No one was willing to step outside of their wrote production script. When he became overwhelmed and quit I was offered his position. I was initially reluctant but found that I could be bought for a few more cents an hour. I simply trained a few key people on the line what things to watch out for and empowered them to fix the issues before they got to me. Things ran so smoothly that I started assisting where there were bottle necks on the line. I didn’t do all of this unselfishly. Although I do enjoy helping others and fixing processes; we worked for piece rate. That meant that the more treadmills we made in a night, the more money we made. With the production line running smooth and everyone energized to make more money, quality control seemed to be the greatest hold up. So, I again was offered a few more cents an hour to work QC. Then after only a few short months, I got to be a lead in charge of a couple people for a special project. I don’t recall exactly what the circumstances were but I do remember one of the people on my team thinking I was too harsh and crying. When the project we were working on ended I went back to quality control and was not asked to continue in a lead position. I enjoyed my job and continued to make good money for me and the team. I learned many lessons from this experience **1- Seek for ways to help the team. Similar to the lesson form a previous story about helping individuals, Look outside of what you are doing to make the organization better. Often times you will be rewarded for your efforts. 2- As a leader, look for those who can make your team or organization better and put them in positions to do so, which may include a promotion. 3- Some people are born leaders, others have to work at it. Either way there are skills and attributes that can be learned. Otherwise books like this one would be useless. I clearly had to work at it. I had the potential. Which I recognize in myself as I look back and think about how I influenced others. But leading is more than motivating. 4- (this lesson came up again) not everyone is ready now but it does not preclude them from being ready later, especially with training or experience. 5- People are often promoted one level higher than their capability. Great workers do not always great leaders make. Give yourself some wiggle room to test those you promote and don’t be afraid to put them back to doing something they are good at or finding ways to use their skills in conventional ways and to reward in unconventional ways. Promotions don’t have to mean being in charge of more people.**

**HIGH GROUND ODYSSEY: SEASON 1 (PART 2)**  
**PHASE 3: THE MISSION (Servant Leadership)**  
**Week 12 - Apr 08: The Shower Strategy**  
_Theme: Blue Ocean Strategy (5AM showers)_  
[Chapter Four: None of it Works Unless You Do]  
I had one goal as a young boy. I grew up in a very religious home. Young men in my faith are expected to serve as missionaries for two years, if they are at all able, at age 19. (Now they can serve at 18. Young women may also serve.) That is what I wanted to do. At eight years old my parents opened a savings account for me and one for my older sister. I saved every bit of the money I earned for that far off mission I would someday serve.  
Receiving one’s mission call is an exciting time. Once an application is submitted candidates wait for their call that could assign them almost anywhere in the world. I was more than excited to learn that I would be at the Missionary Training Center (MTC) for ten weeks to learn Spanish and then off to serve in the Costa Rica, San Jose Mission for two years. As a missionary, I quickly became known as one who followed the rules, even in the MTC. (Missionaries are assigned partners called companions. Male missionaries use the title "Elder" and female missionaries are called "Sister".) It helped immensely to have a companion at the MTC as dedicated to the mission as I was. The first day at the MTC our instructor asked which of us had taken any Spanish classes. The 11 other missionaries in my district raised their hands. I was the only one with no Spanish schooling. This frightened and motivated me. My companion and I would get up at 5:00 am, one hour before the required 6:00 am wake up time. We did this for a couple reasons. We lived on third floor of a building where all missionaries got up at the same time every day. Which meant that, hundreds of people were trying to use the showers at the same time. Besides being crowded, the hot water would quickly run out, especially on the top floor. You could actually hear when the water went cold by floor as the yips moved down the building of those caught with their pants down. Once all these young men showered and dressed, they all went to breakfast… at the same time. The line rapped the building hall, outside, and down the sidewalk. Elder Welling and I discovered that by waking up early, we could enjoy a hot shower in an un-crowded bathroom, get dressed, and be down to breakfast as the doors opened, sparring us the wait in line. This opened the floodgates to not just the hour of extra time for getting up early but almost an entire additional hour to study. And what we felt we needed to study most was Spanish.  
*Time is a limited resource. If you are vigilant you can find better ways to maximize this critical commodity. The program for learning a foreign language at the MTC includes the critical element SYL, Speak Your Language, or HSI, Habla Su Idioma, in Spanish. Elder Welling and I determined to do just that. It was hard, and caused some contention when others were not as strict as us with the program, but ultimately our hard work paid off. Those who expressed that they would learn the language when they got to country were disappointed that it didn’t get any easier. Many of them, even after two years, did not have a command of the language. The best advice I received before entering the MTC was from my good buddy Erik, the guy who talked me into joining the Army. He was older than me by a few months and passed on a few simple, but powerful, words of wisdom in a letter from where he served in the Philippines. "When you leave for the MTC you are not leaving for Costa Rica" he wrote. That seemed obvious enough. He continued "I left so excited to get to the Philippines that I missed some great opportunities to learn." I was fortunate to learn from someone else and I did not share the same regret.  
This is a hard lesson to learn for a motivated leader. Patience is part of it, but it is not simply the ability to wait it out. Leaders must take advantage of situations to the fullest. Corporate training, for example, can appear to be a waste of time when you have "real work" to do. Avoid the urge and tendency to blow it off. Take advantage, it is worth it. The other lesson is that, if you have feedback don’t keep it to yourself. Share it with those to your left and right. If you get feedback, pay attention. Making mistakes is costly. Making mistakes after you are given the answers, is costly and dumb.  
**Week 13 - Apr 15: Misionero Excelente**  
_Theme: High Expectations & The Pygmalion Effect_  
[Chapter Four: Part 2]  
For my first assignment in Costa Rica, I found myself paired with a missionary from Honduras. It is typical for missionaries to have a companion missionary to work alongside. My companion would teach me everything from where to get breakfast and catch the bus, to how to teach and serve with love, but he did not speak English and, as much as I had studied, I truly did not speak Spanish. It would be a challenging experience. In addition to miles of walking and talking to hundreds of people, Elder Ventura drilled me constantly on my gringo accent. “Roca, piedra, turunca,” he repeated. I attempted again and again. Before long, locals began to ask where I was from. I didn’t always understand their questions nor was my vocabulary truly up to the point that I could explain it when I did understand, but my accent had improved to the point that many people were convinced that I was a native Spanish speaker. That is until they heard me speak more and my limited vocabulary and faulty grammar gave me away.  
The President the mission interviewed all of the missionaries one on one every couple of months to gage their wellbeing and provide individual counsel. In my first such interview about a month after I arrived the President started something like this “Elder Sparrow," he said looking me in the eyes, "eres un buen misionaro” (Elder being my missionary title.) “Elder Sparrow, you are a good missionary” he complemented me in Spanish. “Gracias presidente” I said back with my fresh gringo accent, feeling pretty good about myself. He looked at me, squinted his eyes slightly, and brought his eyebrows closer together as he shook his head from side to side “pero no eres un misionaro excelente. Nesesito que seas un misionero excelente. Entiendes?” (”But you are not an excellent missionary. I need you to be an excellent missionary. Do you understand?”) I shook my head up and down. I did understand, but I was devastated. He proceeded to provide me with some specific areas to improve. I understood basic principles but lacked a deeper knowledge of scriptural text and doctrine. I obeyed the mission rules but fell short of keeping my thoughts and actions consistent with a representative of a supreme power. President Gonzalez gave me some specific areas to study and actions to demonstrate maturity. That verbal gut punch motivated me to work harder and be better. My dad had told me in the first letter he wrote to me at the MTC “There are many motivational words of encouragement but none of them work unless you do”. I determined to work hard and become a misionaro excelente.  
Don’t be afraid to let someone know when they need to step up their game. Slackers and high performers alike respond to sincere, directed, constructive feedback. Those who do not respond to constructive feedback fall into the category discussed previously that should be removed from the organization as to not drag down the team. This is of course a balance. I am not at all suggesting the constant pointing out of faults and specifically discourage belittling, yelling, or attempting to motivate through passive aggression. Complements and encouragement are better motivators but we often think that because we see the specific deficiencies that they must be obvious to the one committing them. Shown the path to success most of us will be happy to get on that road.  
**Week 14 - Apr 22: Leading from the Right**  
_Theme: Humility & Leading while following (Elder Searls)_  
[Chapter Four: Part 3]  
I moved to my second area and with five months in the mission field I became the senior companion. My junior companion, Elder Searls, had been there but a month and had disliked much of it. He spoke with a bit of a drawl that came through his budding Spanish. People didn't always understand his words but they could feel the sincerity from his heart the size of Texas, where he was from. On my first day with him I had him show me everyone and everything he knew. At the end of a long day we walked back to the concrete box we called home. As we passed a particular house he said, "I think we should talk to the people at that house". “OK” I said and I walked toward it. “Right now?” he asked astonished. “Did you have another time in mind?” I asked rhetorically. We stood to the gate looking at each other for a bit. I finally said, as if he should have already known, “Well, it’s your house.” He tapped on the gate with a large coin and hollered “Upe”; as is costmary in Costa Rica. A woman came to the door and introduced herself as Joana. Together we set an appointment when we could return to share a message with her. Elder Searls floated home that evening. When his feet did touch the ground, they danced every step. That day I had allowed him to be the teacher, to show me. I hadn’t done it because I read it in a book, which now you have, so you have no excuse not to. I had done it because I was able to admit that, just because I was in charge didn’t mean that I knew everything, which was easy because I knew hardly anything. Not knowing is not weak for a leader. Not learning is weak. And not using the knowledge of those you lead is silly. You will be a better leader when you listen to, and learn from, the wealth of knowledge held by those you lead.  
A month later Elder Searls left the area and Elder Stoker, strait from Washington, arrived. His thick beard that darkened even a freshly shaved face didn't hide how young he was. He wanted desperately to be a good missionary but he was inexperienced, nervous, and very homesick. I had not realized how much basic training had prepared me not to worry too much about being homesick. I never worried much about moving to a new place. I didn't know how to relate. So I did what I knew how to do: work hard, and follow the rules. Elder Stoker and I worked hard. We literally ran at times in slacks and ties to get where we needed to be. That didn’t leave much time for doing much else, including being homesick. I learned that I work harder and I strive to be better when someone is watching and learning from me. This may not be the case for everyone but when I am running alone I am much more prone to slow my pace or shorten the distance without someone there to hold me accountable. I am especially more likely to step up my game if a young person is running with me that I feel is watching me as an example. What I didn’t appreciate at that time was the reality of depression and anxiety. While I am happy it worked out, and I am a big proponent that hard work can solve a lot of problems, it is important to recognize in your subordinates, peers, and even superiors mental states that may require professional assistance.

This can be an especially hard lesson for perfectionists who have a hard time seeing that 100% and 95% are both an A. and the diminishing return of the effort to get one more point may not be worth the cost. Despite all the years of training, months of planning, and weeks of traveling, one can never be fully prepared for the experience of combat. Even for those who have deployed and experienced similar environments before, there is no better lesson than being “on the ground”, and there are no better instructors than the people who carried out the mission for the past year. This changing of the guards is known as a relief in place (RIP). A critical element to the RIP is a right seat/left seat ride. In American automobiles, the steering wheel is on the left side, and so it follows that the individual driving the vehicle sits on the left side and the passenger on the right. Experience has shown that it is beneficial for incoming leaders to sit in the, preverbal, right seat while observing the outgoing leader as they “drive” the mission. Then, at a designated time, the roles are reversed and the incoming leaders and Soldiers take the helm while continuing to receive council, mentorship, and training from the subject matter experts (SME).

**Week 31 - Aug 19: Gunners Up vs. Down**  
_Theme: Analysis Paralysis (The RPG Hit)_  
[Chapter Seven: Part 2]  
Should gunners in the vehicles be up or down? If a gunner is up, in the turret, he runs the risk of being shot or hit by fragmentation from an explosive blast, rocket, or rocket propelled grenade. If a gunner is down, inside the vehicle, he may miss the opportunity to engage the triggerman or deter an attack from happening all together.  
If a vehicle is struck by an IED, should the other members of the patrol rush to their aid, at the risk of exposure to a secondary explosion and the initiation of the actual ambush? or should the patrol cautiously search for secondary devices, at the risk of not aiding a fallen and potentially dyeing comrade?  
As we trained for combat these and other questions came up again and again, and were hotly debated. These questions were fundamental to how we would operate in theater. **Week 32 - Aug 26: The Red Glow Stick**  
_Theme: Risk Mitigation vs. Risk Aversion_  
[Chapter Seven: Part 3]  
For the rest of the night we had it out. That moment was a turning point for me. Of all the lessons learned this lesson fundamentally changed the way I approached everything. The result was a determination to deal with risk deliberately. War is inherently, inevitably dangerous. Route clearance is unavoidably detrimental to one's life expectancy. But hand wave policies to deal with the danger and blanket declarations about how to plan for risk, potentially shift risk and do not actually mitigate it.  
So, should gunners be up? It depends. What is the risk? For the lead truck the greatest risk was a decapitating explosion from an improvised explosive device. Risk that was less present for gunners in the following vehicles especially if the first few trucks did their job of detecting and eliminating that threat. In order to deal with the risk of small arms fire, RPGs, and other external threats, gunners in the remaining vehicles would remain up. This was, of course a general concept but individual gunners were given autonomy to do their job, and mitigate the greatest risk, to the best of their ability.  
Should the other members of the patrol rush to the aid of a damaged vehicle? Again, it depends. We determined that if someone was in danger of their life, no matter the danger we would do all we could to rescue them. That did not mean that we would throw caution to the wind but it did mean we would accept prudent risk to rescue our buddies, whether from our platoon or other forces. We adopted a common, simple solution for communicating when radios were rendered inoperable from the shockwave of a blast. A red glow-stick against the window would show an immediate need for medical attention. Green would signify that rescuers could cautiously search for potential secondary explosions or an ambush prior to approaching the disabled vehicle. The use of a red chem-light was not to be employed lightly, because it meant the risk of others. A broken leg from a blast, for example, may be extremely painful and require medical attention but would not cause loss of life and attention could wait for a deliberate, thorough search.  
What else were we doing in the name of risk mitigation that was actually making our job more difficult and, in turn, actually making it less safe? The operator of the robotic arm on the Buffalo indicated that the bar armor used to mitigate the threat of RPGs blocked his ability to safely disarm IEDs. We analyzed data from thousands of missions and determined that, despite the attack on our lead truck just mentioned, the threat in the specific area in which we operated was very low. But that the risk of not finding an IED or not having the ability to see to disarm it was extremely high; so we removed the bar armor, to the horror of our leaders and peer clearance platoons, who could not understand why we would take off something designed to protect us. To me, it did not matter anymore what something was designed to do, It mattered what it actually did.  
We had sophisticated detection vehicles designed to detect metal in the ground but those vehicles had to be directly over the ordinance in order to detect it, a prospect that seemed counter to the idea of safely finding IEDs. Other teams used the vehicles with great success but we found greater success with other, less conventional, methods.  
Every aspect of every missions was analyzed to assess potential risk, determine the likelihood and severity of the threat, and develop a mitigation strategy.  
This moment defined our culture as a unit. We became adamant that we were the best unit to find a device and if we missed it, heaven forbid, we were the best equipped to take the hit.  
One night, my former Platoon Sergeant cornered me in the company command post. "You're getting pretty reckless out there." He scolded. I prided myself in being open to feedback and willing to adapt. Never did I expect the visceral response I felt from being called reckless. Were we unorthodox? Yes. Aggressive? Yes! Affective? Absolutely. But reckless? Absolutely not. Is route clearance dangerous? 100%. Is it risky? Not if mitigated properly.  
I do admit that we didn't balance well the long term risk to our force with the risk to our mission and the result was a culture of walking off things that probably required medical attention. It was a heavy price to pay for dedication to mission accomplishment and something that I still struggle with, recognizing that I should have done more to look after the long term effects of concussive blasts on my team.  
Leaders must make decisions. That is what you are paid to do. There are plenty of smart talented capable people, who can do their job without you or even do your job better than you. None of that matters. You are in that position and you are the decision maker. If there is anything that sets you apart from anyone else with or without a fancy title, you have to make choices.  
The solution to a problem is rarely binary, one or the other, but a combination of the two or an answer outside of what either party considers.  
I want to be clear about our tactics and operating procedures. Some of what we implemented is well known or common sense. Some of what we did was not the right way for other successful teams to operate. The important thing is that it worked for us, at that time, in that situation. The idea is not to glean specific procedures but an overall better assessment of risk, how to avoid risk aversion while mitigating potential dangers.  
We cannot be afraid to try. Bounce back with a can do attitude.

We left FOB Falcon tired but with heads high, and headed toward FOB Stryker for a short pitstop on the way home, well, home being Camp Ramadi. I could not help reflecting on the last two months. In June of 2007 my platoon had been selected from the nine platoons in the Battalion to go to Bagdad and assist with the troop surge. Our mission had not been entirely clear. I knew we were going East, but that was all I knew. Our first Sergeant, was concerned about how we would be received in Bagdad. He demanded that on the day we left we all line up while he inspected everyone to ensure they had on all their protective gear. The day arrived with all the scrambling and coordination to get a platoon ready for a trip across the country to an area we knew nothing about. First Sergeant came out for his inspection. He was disappointed to see that not everyone had on the gear he required of them. It mattered not that we would miss our scheduled departure time and not arrive until late into the night. He sent us to get all of our gear and put it on which, embarrassingly, took several hours.  
I pause here to make two observations: One, enforcement of standards is an extremely important principal, particularly in the military. I respect the First Sergeant and his enforcement of standards at that moment. However there is a dichotomy, which Jocko Willink better addresses in his book "The Dichotomy of Leadership", that standards have to be balanced with flexibility.  
As addressed in the risk chapter, leaders must be certain that in our attempt to enforce standards we are not causing the very thing we are trying to prevent. Like when the zealous Zeng meets his destiny on his way to avoid it, providing Tai Lung the ability to escape in his very attempt to verify that he could not escape.(yes that is a Kung Fu Panda reference )  
The other reason I mention it is to simply explain why we arrived so late and frustrated with where we had just come from.  
We arrived in the early hours of the morning, tired of traveling, tired of trying to find our way, just plain tired. As we pulled into a large gravel parking lot, not sure where to go, we saw the iconic red castle, symbol of Army Engineers, a welcome sight. A company First Sergeant in the 875th Engineer Battalion from Arkansas welcomed us almost immediately, as if he had waited a long time to greet us. He showed us the way to the motor pool and to our tent. He brought out boxes of Pop Tarts and bottles of Gatorade apologizing that he had so little to give just then, pointing out the chow hall and the dining hours. Dry sugary pastries and warm salty electrolytes never tasted so good. We were stunned and not accustomed to being treated with such hospitality. After a couple hours of rest I made my way to the motor pool. Soldiers for my platoon Were already up and working. I’m sure some of them hadn’t slept. They ran up to me with the biggest grins. "Sir, you know those lights we’ve been trying to get for the whole time we’ve been in Iraq." "yah’, I nodded, confused. "These guys are putting them on our trucks." They nearly skipped back to the motor pool with me like giddy school girls. I soon saw why. "How did you find anything without good lights?" A mechanic asked rhetorically, shaking his head at me as I watched him and his team crawling all over our trucks like skilled spider monkeys, upgrading alternators, changing wiring, and replacing hydraulics. I thanked him genuinely. He apologized that they couldn't do more.  
From there, I made my way to the 875th Tactical Operations Center, an unremarkable building filled with remarkable people. I met the Battalion Commander, LTC Anslow, a short, fiery, lady with a brilliant mind and a friendly smile who reminded me of my mother, tough but caring. For the next month and a half the 875th took care of everything we requested of them and treated us like family. I had never been to Arkansas but when LTC Anslow handed me a Razor Back patch as we left and wished us well, I removed the patch from my own unit and replaced it with hers. (I carry that patch in my uniform pocket to this day to remember the power of a caring leader.)  
We returned to Ramadi triumphantly, still on a bit of an adrenaline rush from a dangerous mission, still on a high from the awards and accolades we just received from strangers turned family, excited to be back with our unit. A few Soldiers came out to greet us, including our company commander MAJ T and First Sergeant. As the Battalion commander and Operations Officer passed by it became immediately obvious that it was a chance passing. "I expect a full report from you," was the only thing he said too me as he passed, before turning to the operations officer and directing "Let's go". As they moved out in the direction of the chow hall the contrast between these and the leaders I just left could not have been more stark.  
I rapped sharply on the Battalion Commander's door prepared with mission statistics to back brief our time away from the flagpole. I wore on my right shoulder the patch of the third infantry division. On my left sleeve I sported the diamond shaped patch with the razor back symbol of the Arkansas National Guard. He noticed immediately. "Take that patch off of him." he angrily directed the Command Sergeant Major who was already in his office when I approached and standing directly to my left across the desk from the commander. The Command Sergeant Major shook his head at me disappointed and ripped the patch off my sleeve with dramatic effect visually and aurally with the sound of the Velcro tearing away. "And I want to see orders authorizing that patch." he glared at the other sleeve. I extended my hand with the paper already highlighted, prepared for the encounter. He didn't even glace at it and refused to take it. The report was over before it started. He was already obviously involved in a conversation with the Command Sergeant Major before I showed up to be a nuisance. I got a lecture about how I worked for him, and whatever vacation I had taken was over, and I could get out of his office. It had been an unnecessary provoking stunt. I wore the patches as a statement and I got the reaction I was looking for. I was fortunate not to be reprimanded more severely.  
But I felt justified. ”Vacation”? Yup, I had just been chillin’ at the beach in my board shorts. I’m still a little too bitter to flesh out the point of my thoughts. This particular commander had been an expert in all things Army Engineer before the current conflict but many things had changed. I and my fellow platoon leaders, we were the experts. We had been to the Clearance courses and we had the experience of living it outside the wire every day. I think he was a little intimidated by that (side note don’t be concerned if you are a leader of people who know more than you, that’s a good thing you have a different role as leader let them be the experts.)  
bottom line On this one, caring goes a long way.  
**Week 45 - Nov 25: The Volleyball Net**  
_Theme: THANKSGIVING. Victory/Peace in Ramadi._  
[Chapter Twelve: Don’t Forget to Enjoy It and Leave a Legacy]  
I served as a route clearance platoon leader in Ramadi, Iraq from 2006-2007. I couldn’t wear a memorial bracelets for all the people I cared about who we lost because my arm and my heart wouldn’t take it. We got hit by so many IEDs I lost count. But, I will never forget when we drove into downtown Ramadi after we had finally cleared the last of the insurgency out of the city. As the lead vehicle rounded a corner out of my sight and faced up the street designated to be cleared, the vehicle commander radioed back to me in confusion and concern. “6,1, they’ve got a volleyball net blocking the road up here." He paused, then asked "What should we do?” He was as serious as I had ever heard him. It was a hilarious question from a guy who fought to be aggressive and not be risk averse, who insisted we put gunners out in the turrets where they could engage the enemy, who had been on the ground and in culverts to find IEDs, who would rush though a hail storm of gunfire to the aid of his comrades The same SSG Hale who said "let me do my job and find IEDs" and "If you don't trust me fire me." It highlights the absurdity of the situation, but mostly, it highlights the depth of our engagement with the enemy, previously any road block or intentional delay had been a set up for an ambush, on this day there would be no ambush, it was people genuinely enjoying themselves. It highlights for me the drastic change we helped propagate and the privileges that was ours to witness victory.  
I called back “1, can you ask them to please move it, over.” “They’re moving it 6, we’ll be moving in a minute.” A few minutes later he came back over the radio. “6, we’re not gonna’ to find any IEDs. There are people everywhere.” I had heard that Ramadi was a city of 200,000 people but I hadn’t seen very many of them. Those that I did see were usually eliminated as a threat. Now droves of people were everywhere, smiling and waving and we were right in the middle of a big parade. In that moment some of the sacrifice seemed validated, at least in some small way for me.  
**Week 46 - Dec 02: Attitude**  
_Theme: Parenting & Stoicism_  
[Chapter Twelve: Part 2]  
As a new father, I thought that I just had to figure out the trick to parenting. I imagined that once I knew the secret I could just put that on repeat until they turned 18 and moved out and I would be good to go. I mean, humans have been parenting children for thousands of years. Surely somebody figured it out by now, I just had to find the magic “three step process”. Unfortunately that’s not a thing. Whenever there are humans involved there are variables and the goal posts keep moving.  
Industries change, rewards change, what motivates people changes, over time the world changes. If you’re not learning and adapting with it, you are not leading. Leaders have to anticipate and it is impossible to anticipate without continuing to study work at it and learn  
A doctor who leaves medical school thinking they know everything there is to know about medicine is sadly mistaken. That doctor can’t even know all that is currently known about medicine, Let alone everything that will be discovered about medicine in the future. that person may be able to practice for a time but will eventually fall behind and fail in their profession.  
Same for leaders, keep learning and keep working at it. Effort is recognized.  
There are things I’ve learned in life that took me a lifetime to understand. What I didn’t know I was learning in preschool was to enjoy life. The learning and working hard comes easier when we are enjoying ourselves. If it’s drudgery then you are not understanding the purpose of the task or you are not finding that spoon full of sugar. There are countless books on positive attitude. It seems the world is all on Prozac, some people need to get it. Others just need to get off their behinds and enjoy life. If you are in a profession you do not enjoy, maybe you should look for the exit. Money means a lot, but it is not everything. Career is a lot, but failing at home is a hefty price to pay for a successful career and leaves some wealthy and empty. Balance life. As a leader it is important that your employees are able to balance their lives too. Don’t forget the individuals that make up your teams, if they are not happy they are harder to lead.  
“The longer I live, the more I realize the impact of attitude on life. Attitude, to me, is more important than facts. It is more important than the past, than education, than money, than circumstances, than failures, than successes, than what other people think or say or do. It is more important than appearance, giftedness or skill. It will mak3e or break a company...a church....a home. The remarkable thing is, we have a choice every day regarding the attitude we will embrace for that day. We cannot change our past...we cannot change the fact that people 4will act in a certain way. We cannot change the inevitable. The only thing we can do is play on the one string we have, and that is our attitude...I am convinced that life is 10% what happens to me and 90% how I react to it. And so it is with you...we are 5in charge of our attitudes.”  
― Charles R. Swindoll  
**Week 47 - Dec 09: The End of the Odyssey**  
_Theme: Sacrifice & Legacy_  
[Chapter Twelve: Part 3]  
It is my grand hope that whomever reads these words will recognize that I have done little right, to the contrary, maybe the only thing I have done right is having a growth mindset and continuing to learn and preserver. I aspire to be a great leader, in truth I'm barley a mediocre father and probably just squeak past lousy to be an average husband. Even my own lessons, I forget. I fail, and return like a dog to it's vomit again and again. I see others who are stronger, smarter, and much more capable than I, but I warn that, envy, pride, self loathing, and bitterness are enemies to happiness. In contrast, gratitude, humility, acknowledgement, and learning are seeds to fulfillment and joy.  
Before I close, one point I want to make: Getting to where I am took hard work and sacrifice. Sometimes at the expense of the family I love more than any awards or recognition or accomplishments I have made. Don’t forget the sacrifices your people make. Sacrifice to be a leader. I also make this last point so as not to bury it deep inside some forgettable monolog or story. Please never forget that there are people behind the people who work for you. They are all different, but don’t forget the families.  
I am amazed everyday by how much difference leadership, or lack of leadership, makes in organizations and in the lives of people. Leadership Matters!  
So we end where we began. Learn who you are. Learn your history. Use your talents and experiences to leave a legacy. Be the leader you want to see.  
The book has to end at some point. And here is as good a place as any. Life will keep going and when I die others will follow and carry on the story. I believe that in some other state we to will carry on to new experiences and more learning.  
“If you want a happy ending, that depends, of course, on where you stop your story.” - Orson Welles
