**Risk Aversion vs Recklessness (Ignoring Risk)**  
In the 2002 movie ==“Reign of Fire”,== dragons have taken over the earth and Quinn (played by Christian Bale) leads a community surviving in a castle in the English country side. Quinn’s survival skills and leadership have kept his little colony alive when most of the rest of the world has fallen victim to the dragons. His fear of dragons is based on the death of his mother to the first discovered dragon, which he witnessed, highlighted at the beginning of the movie. Ironically his aversion to risk that kept them alive for so long may ultimately lead to the colony’s destruction as they are running out of food and leaving to harvest is dangerous. The call and response “prayer” that they “pray” as they go to bed each night illustrates Quinn’s fear and associated risk aversion -  
“What do we do when we wake?”, Quinn asks.. To which the children reply,  
“Keep one eye on the sky.”  
"What do we do when we sleep?”  
"Keep one eye on the sky.”  
"What do we do when we see them?”  
“Dig hard, dig deep, run for shelter, and don’t look back.”  
In stark contrast, Denton Van Zan (played by Matthew McConaughey) is an American military man with a tank, a helicopter, and the writers didn’t think that was enough for his stereo type so they added a giant battle ax for him to carry. These crazy military American types have one guy free fall from a helicopter as dragon bate while two others free fall behind him with net guns to try to catch a dragon.  
Just when you think this far-fetched fantasy can’t get fetched any farther, it gets a tad more unrealistic as (spoiler alert) Van Zan leaps from atop a tall structure at the giant male dragon with his battle axe glinting in the twilight, despite all of humanities nuclear arsenals having been no match for the dragons. The dragon scores one last victory as it chomps McConaughey out of the air (definitely the highlight of the film) before finally meeting his demise by taking an explosive arrow to the face.  
It’s a ridiculous movie. But highlights nicely the contrast, and the danger, of both ignoring risk and being to averse to it. The colony in the English castle is running out of food and will starve to death without devising some way to deal with the dragons. The Americans, in contrast, are ultimately successful but at great loss. The solution, then, is a way to acknowledge danger, identity the actual hazards, and develop a viable strategy to mitigate the risk.  
**Risk Management**  
Army Doctrine for risk management includes five steps.  
Step #1, **Identify the Hazards**: In other words, determine the things that could go wrong. For some, there is not much on that list, "Nothing could go wrong" they pretend. To them, a word of caution: It is not a matter of 'if' but 'what' and 'when'. Even the luckiest of us will get ill, get crashed into by someone else, or the air-conditioner will give out on the hottest day. Something will always go sideways. While it is useful to err on the side of a more exhaustive tabulation of even unlikely hazards and the more potential hazards are identified the more likely they can be planned for or anticipated, this can be taken to the extreme For some people there could be an infinitely exhaustive list of what-ifs. What if the sky falls? What if a zombie apocalypse breaks out? What if there is a sharknato? To those people, a question, What if it all works out? The list of hazards can include everything from a tiny splinter to a coordinated terrorist attack. At a minimum it is critical to identify the actual most likely bad thing that could happen and the actual potentially worst thing that could happen.  
It is important in this step to identify the actual hazard, for example, falling from a tall building is not the hazard, it's the sudden stop at the bottom that hurts. I make the distinction here because mitigating the hazard may be preventing the fall in the first place, which may be the best solution, but it may not be an toption, and it excludes viable mitigations such as slowing the fall, like with a parachute, or cushioning the fall with a net, neither of which prevent the fall but do mitigate the actual hazard of being injured at the bottom.  
One more note here about hazards here. Mostly we think of risk in terms of risk to people. The bodily harm that could occur is the vital first hazard to consider. We can expand our aperture to include risk to assets, things and stuff. And, In addition to the risk to force just described, we should also consider the risk to mission. We should consider the hazards that are preventing us from reaching the objective? Let's say I have a goal to run in the morning to get fit. I assess that I might trip and fall or pull a muscle, These are viable hazards to Identify. But if my mitigation turns out to be staying home, then it's not a viable solution because it risks the accomplishment of the mission.  
Step #2 **Assess the Hazards:**  
a. Probability. Determine how likely it is for the identified hazard to happen. While it is helpful to identify the spectrum of hazards, Step 2 allows the risk of a vampire bite to be binned neatly into the 'not very likely' category, where less time, effort, and resources will be expended in its mitigation.  
b. Severity. Conjecture how catastrophic it would be if the event occurred; how bad would it be? While freezing your tongue to a flag pole is uncomfortable and potentially embracing, it can be remedied fairly quickly with warm water. Shooting your eye out, on the other hand, could be devastating.  
c. Aggregate the likelihood, from step 2a, with the severity, in step 2b, to calculate the overall risk, often lumped into categories of low, medium, high, or extremely high. If the worst thing that could happen is that sharknato we mentioned, and the likelihood is pretty much zero, then the overall risk is probably low or at most medium even if the potential damage could be quite severe.  
Step #3 **Develop Controls and Make Risk Decisions**: This is to two part step  
a. Create Controls (ways to eliminate or reduce risks). For some, this comes naturally, Those people are thinking about how to avoid risk before they fully identify the hazards, not recommended. Make sure you are creating solutions to actual problems you have. It is criticalto understand  
b. Decide which controls to implement, accepting only prudent risks. Step 3 involves assessing the residual risk after implementing the controls. Even after you put on your helmet, reducing the potentiality and severity of a head injury, you can still get hurt. And because it is impossible to eliminate all risk, this step includes a leader deciding how much risk is acceptable. The leader underwrites the risk. They are responsible to answer for injury, damage, mission failure, etc.  
Step #4 **Implement Controls**: This is probably the most important step, although obviously made possible by the previous steps, it does no good to check some box to create a risk assessment if nothing is done about it. Chosen controls should be put into action during all parts of the operation, planning, preparation, and execution. Don't say you're going to do it if you know it won't happen. If you don't actually mitigate the risk, then the process is useless.  
Step #5 **Supervise and Evaluate**: Continuously monitor the situation, check if controls are working, and adjust as needed
 
Exploring bold or unusual ideas can lead to surprising breakthroughs, but that means taking risks. Next, you’ll discover how incremental changes mitigate risk.

Let's say you need to pick up groceries from the store. So you jump into your forest green 1970's AMC Gremlin and put on your, newly required, seatbelt. That seatbelt is a mitigation for the potential hazard of wrecking that beautiful car. As a point of order, It must be mentioned that this strategy only mitigates against the potential bodily harm of a vehicle accident, it does not protect the vintage car from structural damage. You could avoid the risk of an accident altogether by not going to the store at all, but that doesn’t solve your unquenchable need for rice crispy treats. Other mitigations include: when you drive, how fast you drive, avoiding distractions while you drive, and getting enough sleep before you drive. You can help others not hit you by turning on your lights, using a turn signal, and driving defensively. Manufacturers design cars with accident mitigation measures including airbags, back up cameras, and even smart driving technologies that reduce risk. Engineers design and build road networks with mitigation in mind. On and on, This is not meant to be an accident avoidance course but many are familiar with the mitigations for avoiding injury as a result of a automobile accident. All of this effort and billions of dollars and people still are hurt and people we know die. That doesn’t stop or shouldn't stop us from getting out there, but it should give us pause and cause us to properly and effectively mitigate risk.
