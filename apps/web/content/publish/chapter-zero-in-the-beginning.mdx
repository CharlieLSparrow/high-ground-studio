# Chapter Zero — In the Beginning

## Homer — In the Beginning

My great-grandfather’s father immigrated to the United States from Australia in the late 1800s. His father migrated to Australia from England in an era when most people from England associated Australia with criminals. I continue to search for the reasons he made that voyage.

What I do know is that his descendants — my grandfather and his father — were tough men.

During the Great Depression of the 1930s, when despair and tragedy crippled the nation, my great-grandfather, Charles Sparrow, was not selling — he was buying. He acquired a significant stretch of ground in an arid valley in Southeastern Idaho, which he cleared of sagebrush and rocks with the help of good horses and four sons.

In the 1940s, when his other sons received agricultural deferments to stay out of the war, one boy — my grandfather, Raymond Sparrow — did not defer and was drafted into the Army.

Private Sparrow was in France guarding prisoners of war when, in an escape attempt, one of the prisoners shanked him in the abdomen with a jagged piece of metal. While recovering aboard a hospital ship, Army leaders discovered that this Idaho farm kid could weld. He then spent the rest of the war welding ships.

He returned home with little fanfare to a family farm where brothers had already staked claims. Rather than cause contention, he sought employment elsewhere. There were plenty of infrastructure projects in those days, and his skills as a welder would serve him well.

My aunt, in later years, presented me with parts of his Army uniform including his “Eisenhower” jacket, which I consider today to be one of my most prized possessions.

I never asked Grandpa Sparrow about his time in World War II. I never asked him much about anything, despite working side by side with him almost daily on the farm growing up. What I relayed here I know only from the little my dad shared with me after my grandfather passed away.

I pause from storytelling at this point to impart some of the little wisdom I have, as I will do throughout the book.

The second “to do” — the first being to look for the extraordinary in the ordinary and tell your own story — is to find out who you are and where you came from.

I know it’s not earth-shattering advice, and most readers will determine by now whether to read on or add this to the stack of books they started to read but never “got into.” Also, probably already obvious, I have a lot of learning to do myself, as I don’t know why my third-great-grandfather went to Australia in the first place.

But truth is true regardless of who it comes from and how simple it might be.

The power of self-reflection does not cease to amaze me.

Knowing who you are helps establish a starting point. It provides a lens through which to look out into the future and determine where it is you want to be. Many self-help and leadership books start here, with creating a vision. I agree that knowing where you are going is invaluable.

I add: it starts with understanding where you are.

And where you are starts with knowing where you came from.

Your personal backstory, even your setbacks, are all part of the setup for who you can become. The first lesson, spoiler alert, is tied inseparably to the last. If you understand the power of the past, you also understand the power you can have on the future — the power of leaving a legacy.

I illustrate by sharing some of the legacy of my Grandpa Raymond’s bride, my Grandma MarDene Sparrow.

She married at age sixteen and had six kids in an era and place where horses and wagons still took the family to church. She was a woman of conviction, her devotion evident as she sang praises. She sang with an amazing soprano voice. She knew the words to all the hymns and didn’t have to be seated on the choir benches to be heard over the congregation.

Grandma Sparrow left a legacy of combining these two ideas of finding motivational stories in everyday life and remembering the stories of the past.

In her own words, the following is the story of **Chub and Jack**.

> In his later years Daddy had a saying above his desk, “Every man in his lifetime is entitled to one good wife, one good horse and one good dog.” Daddy had each of these in his life and each could be the subject for many stories and experiences. Humorous, serious, faith promoting or character building.
>
> My subject for this story is Daddy’s team, Cub and Jack. They are a legend in our family.
>
> When the folks were first married they bought a piece of land at the mouth of Mink Creek. It was 60 acres with a good water right. Much of it was covered with sage brush and willow. Daddy, with the help of this choice team of horses, set to work to move a little two room house to the property and then to clear the land.
>
> Chub and Jack were well matched, and they were obedient. Daddy described them as being “a good honest team.” He said they worked together and he never asked them to pull but what they gave him all they had.
>
> It was the custom for the men to get their winter wood out of Strawberry Canyon. Several men would go together, taking their teams and helping each other. One day while they were cutting and loading their logs, a snow slide came down the mountain and blocked the road.
>
> When the men began their return home some had gone ahead of Dad. They came to the slide and each tried to go through it but their teams balked. Not one could get his team to pull through the slide. Then they said, “We’ll wait for Bill, his team will pull through.”
>
> Sure enough Chub and Jack pulled through. Equal to the faith Daddy had in them. They gave the task all they had, and working together, being obedient to their beloved master, they pulled through the snow slide. Then the other teams could follow for the trail was broke.
>
> You know, a team can be a couple, a family, or a group of people any size, with a common purpose or goal. We can pull through the snow slides of life if we are obedient to the master, work together as a team, and give the effort all we’ve got.
>
> I want to be like Chub and Jack. I want to be like Daddy’s team. I want to “pull through” any problem, any snow slide that might block my road. I want to pull through with my testimony still firm, my faith and love for my Savior still strong. I know the Lord loves me and he expects me to pull through.

After telling that story, one critic muttered, “Cool, a feel-good story about horses. I don’t have horses for ancestors and I don’t relate to your Christian overtone.”

To which I respond: this story is about who I am because of where I came from and sets the stage for where I am going.

Take this invitation and find out where you came from, who you are, and where you are going.

In addition, some principles are universal and can be internalized for our benefit regardless of whether they happened to unrelated people or even animals, such as hard work, overcoming obstacles, trust, and teamwork.

My grandma died of breast cancer while I was at Army Basic Combat Training. She remained strong and kind to the end, always serving her family and her community. I miss her dearly. I never met anyone who loved their birthday as much as she did. April 16th will forever be celebrated in my house in honor of her legacy.

> “And he shall turn the heart of the fathers to the children and the heart of the children to the fathers…”
>
> — Malachi 4:6

The legend of Chub and Jack runs deeper than the solitary conquering of a snow bank.

While assisting with the construction of the Twin Lakes Canal, the entrance to a tunnel collapsed, trapping Jack inside. Grandpa Hansen rushed to breach a hole through which he passed food and water for weeks while men and horses cleared dirt and rocks to reopen the tunnel and free the trapped animal.

Jack was eventually rescued from the tomb where he was nearly buried alive, but not without lasting effects. The weeks in near pitch-black darkness left him ultra-sensitive to light and nearly blind. He relied heavily on the commands of his handler and the feeling of the teammate next to him to know where to walk and when to pull.

Chub remained a loyal companion, guiding him around the yard to avoid obstacles, to food, and to water.

And Jack didn’t quit.

Despite his disability he remained useful, helpful, and strong.

My connection to this tale is deeply personal, as I face my own challenges and rely heavily on my teammates to, sometimes, literally guide me through. I will spare the details at this point because some are yet too raw to share, but I challenge you to derive your own interpretation and meaning from the story, if you haven’t already.

The lessons from these two are endless and legendary.

## Charlie

Once, when I asked my brother about the expert marksmanship badge on his uniform, he confessed that he maintained it in spite of his vision being so poor that he could barely see the far targets.

He compensated by becoming quick and accurate at close range, and in a firefight he appreciates having a marksman with keen eyes to watch for threats beyond his range.

In this book it’s my job to strive to be a good battle buddy to him, and as he tells the story of his life, to look out to the rest of the world and find similar ideas, stories, studies, and statistics to help back him up and strengthen his position.

But in this first — or zeroth — chapter, I first want to put in my two cents with another story we share in our heritage, and then I promise to do my job and tell you, backed up by research, why I think Homer’s advice to “find out who you are and where you came from” may be some of his best in the whole book.

When our Grandpa William “Tony” Francis was young, he was told in a blessing that he would live to a ripe old age.

Later, as a radio operator aboard the U.S.S. *Rooks*, a kamikaze pilot was spotted diving for their ship. As Grandpa Tony told it, and now many of his progeny tell it, in that moment he thought to himself:

> “Well Francis, it looks like you’re going to have to swim tonight.”

As a kid I don’t think I was ever told what the moral of the story was supposed to be, but I am even more in awe of it now than I was hearing about it as a kid.

In my mind I see the words coming from the handsome face I’ve seen in pictures of him wearing his naval uniform proudly. I see him on a battleship of the Pacific fleet doing his duty with optimism and hope even as doom and fear loomed large.

I also see it sitting at the feet of a warm, barrel-chested old man as his face gleamed with love, his lips curved with joy, his eyes filled with tears, and his voice choked with emotion. Most of my memories of my grandpa are of him smiling even as his voice would catch while telling a story or sharing something important to him.

His optimism, strength, and warmth give me strength whenever I think of him, tell his stories, or sing his songs.

Some of my favorite research ever done anywhere ever is that done with the “Do You Know?” scale, and other studies it has inspired. Essentially, children are given a list of twenty yes-or-no questions asking them if they know things about their parents, grandparents, and family history. The results are consistently amazing. Scores on the exam are strongly correlated with higher self-esteem, a more internal locus of control, and lower anxiety.

This isn’t just one of those good-for-kids principles either. A group of university students who were studied while taking a family history class showed an 8% improvement in self-esteem and a 20% reduction in anxiety, with increased family identification acting as a pathway that helped explain broader psychological well-being improvements.

Telling heroic, dramatic, emotional, heartfelt, silly, fun, funny, ridiculous, and even apocryphal family and personal stories has been shown to have dramatic benefits.

Tell your personal, family, and even found-family stories whenever you get a chance. Ask others to share their stories with you. Watch how it affects you, those you love, and those you lead.

And whenever you get a chance, we’d love to hear your stories too.
